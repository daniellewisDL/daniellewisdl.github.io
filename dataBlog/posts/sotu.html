The state of our union is … still getting simpler

How the linguistic standard of the presidential address has kept declining - now through 2026

An update of the Guardian US interactive team's 2013 piece, "the state of our union is … dumber." Reading level scored with the Flesch-Kincaid test. Source texts: stateoftheunion.onetwothree.net.

All 240 annual messages since George Washington in 1790, each scored for the U.S. school grade a reader needs to follow it — one bubble per address (bigger = longer). The slide the Guardian charted in 2013 hasn't stopped: the four simplest addresses in 235 years all came in the last four.

What's changed since the 2013 Guardian piece

  • A new floor. The Guardian's most readable president was George H.W. Bush at grade 8.6. Joseph Biden's addresses now average 7.8, and his 2023 message scored 7.3 — the simplest in the entire 235-year record.
  • The decline didn't deepened. Six of the ten most readable addresses ever now come from the post-2013 era.
  • Trump's two-act arc. His first-term addresses sat near grade 9.3–10.4. His 2025 and 2026 addresses dropped to 7.7–7.8: simpler, looser, and far longer.
  • Simpler and longer. The 2026 address ran 10,588 words — a modern spoken-era record — even as its reading level hit a near-low. Short sentences, more of them.
  • It's not just reading level. Switch the Measure menu above: presidents now say “I” more than ever (Biden 2024 hit 34 per 1,000 words), address the room as “you” far more than the 19th-century page ever did, and pack in short, applause-ready lines — ~38–39% of recent sentences run eight words or fewer, versus near zero for most of history.
  • Two more ways to look. The Length × reading level view (the View switch) shows recent addresses landing in the “longer & simpler” corner; and the 1913 marker on the timeline is where written messages to Congress gave way to the spoken address.

Method. Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level computed with the textstat library over the full text of each address (calibrated to within ~0.5 grades of the Guardian's 2013 figures — e.g. Benjamin Harrison 1892: 15.1 here vs 15.5 there). Bubble area is proportional to word count. "Spoken vs written" before 1913 follows the historical record; a handful of post-1913 written messages (e.g. Coolidge/Hoover, Carter 1981) are flagged best-effort.
