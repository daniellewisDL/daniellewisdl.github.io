†The Office begs to explain the dagger: all correspondence intended for Mr Dickens is routed through this Department, which was beforehand with all the public departments in the art of perceiving — HOW NOT TO DO IT. Members of the public wishing to know, you know, are invited to complete the form below, and to keep their expectations in the family vault.

Regulations for Applicants
1. No enquiry can be received without Form B. — 2. Form B is issued only upon presentation of Form A. — 3. Form A has been discontinued. — 4. The Office congratulates itself upon the elegance of the system.

Persons named Barnacle or Stiltstalking may proceed directly to the head of the queue, the pension list, and the peerage.

Applicants are advised that persistence is futile, and that persistence is also the only thing that has ever worked. Submit repeatedly and see.

Alternative channels

By post: Gad's Hill Place, Higham by Rochester, Kent. Letters are answered by return; the author has views on people who don't.

In person: the author may be overtaken on the Dover road any afternoon, travelling at four and a half miles per hour. Applicants are advised he does not slow down and you will have about six miles of interview.

By séance: the management is aware that a Brooklyn medium published two further volumes of Edwin Drood "dictated by the author's spirit" in 1873. The management has read them. The author's spirit wishes it stated for the record that he would sooner have dictated them to the raven.

The Growlery

Complaints of a general nature against the world may be lodged in the Growlery, per Mr Jarndyce's excellent system: state that the wind is in the east, sit with the complaint until it improves, and on no account allow it to reach Chancery, where it will outlive your grandchildren and consume the estate in costs.

Complaints about the two endings of Great Expectations are received in the Growlery on the last Friday of every month, jointly addressed to Mr Bulwer-Lytton, who started it.
