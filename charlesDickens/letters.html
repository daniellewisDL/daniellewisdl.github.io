The Correspondence · Blue Ink · Answered by Return of Post

Fourteen thousand letters

He answered nearly everything — editors, duchesses, aspiring authors, strangers with grievances — in a racing blue-ink hand, signed with a flourish that used more ink than some men's whole letters. What follows is a sampling of the correspondence, freely rendered after the originals. What does not follow is explained by the bonfire.

⚠ The Bonfire at Gad's Hill, September 1862*
One autumn afternoon the author carried every letter he had ever received — from Thackeray, Tennyson, Carlyle, Collins, everyone — into the field behind the house and burned the lot, basket after basket, while his sons roasted onions in the embers. "Would to God every letter I had ever written was on that pile," he said, as the rain began. He meant it: he had watched private letters of other great men sold and gossiped over, and declined to leave the same feast behind. His side of every correspondence survived, in fourteen thousand pieces, because the recipients felt otherwise.

Devonshire Terrace, to John Forster — c. 1847
My dear Forster,

You ask about the fragment I gave you, and whether I mean to go on with it. I hardly know how I have written even so much. No word of that part of my childhood has ever passed my lips — not to Kate, not to anyone. Even now, famous and caressed and happy, I often forget in my dreams that I have a dear wife and children, even that I am a man, and wander desolately back to that time of my life.

I know how all these things have worked together to make me what I am: but the deep remembrance of the sense I had of being utterly neglected and hopeless — of the shame I felt in my position at the blacking warehouse — cannot be written. I shall put it all into Copperfield instead, where it may do some good, and where no one need know whose story it is.

Ever affectionately,
CD.

Forster kept the secret until his biography of 1872–74, when a stunned public learned that the great celebrant of childhood had spent his own pasting labels in a rat-infested warehouse at twelve, while his father sat in the Marshalsea. Readers of David Copperfield had known for twenty years without knowing they knew.

To Miss Angela Burdett-Coutts — 1846, on the founding of Urania Cottage
My dear Miss Coutts,

I have no doubt whatever that the plan will answer, if it be conducted on one principle above all: that these young women are not to be preached at from a height, but treated as reclaimable creatures with histories, tempers, and futures. There must be order in the house, but there must be cheerfulness too — music, I would say, and bright colours, and no dress of penitential drab. Nobody was ever reformed by being made to look like a punishment.

Each case I will examine myself. We shall keep a book — a history of every one, taken down from her own lips — and we shall ask each of them not "What have you been?" but "What do you mean to be?" Emigration afterwards, a new country and no old neighbours, gives the answer room to be true.

Ever faithfully yours,
Charles Dickens

For over a decade he ran Urania Cottage, a home in Shepherd's Bush for women leaving prostitution and prison, in exactly this hands-on fashion — interviewing candidates in gaol himself, choosing the wallpaper, auditing the accounts, hiring and firing matrons. Roughly a hundred women passed through to new lives in Australia and Canada. He never once wrote about it for publication.

To the Editor of The Times — 13th November 1849, after the hanging of the Mannings at Horsemonger Lane Gaol
Sir,

I was a witness of the execution at Horsemonger Lane this morning. I went there with the intention of observing the crowd, and I stand astounded and appalled by the wickedness it exhibited. A sight so inconceivably awful as the wickedness and levity of the immense crowd could be imagined by no man, and could be presented in no heathen land under the sun.

The horrors of the gibbet and of the crime which brought the wretched murderers to it faded in my mind before the atrocious bearing, looks, and language of the assembled spectators. When the sun rose brightly it gilded thousands upon thousands of upturned faces, so inexpressibly odious in their brutal mirth or callousness, that a man had cause to feel ashamed of the shape he wore.

I am, Sir, your faithful servant,
Charles Dickens

Substantially his actual words. Thirty thousand people attended that hanging as entertainment; vendors sold pies. His letters to the Times helped build the case that ended public execution in Britain in 1868 — one of the few reforms he campaigned for and lived to see carried.

Gad's Hill Place — September 1868, to his youngest son Edward ("Plorn"), aged 16, departing to Australia
My dearest Plorn,

I write this note to-day because your going away is much upon my mind, and because I want you to have a few parting words from me to think of now and then at quiet times. I need not tell you that I love you dearly, and am very, very sorry in my heart to part with you.

I put a New Testament among your books, for the same reasons and with the same hopes that made me write an easy account of it for you when you were a little child — because it is the best book that ever was or will be known in the world, and because it teaches you the best lessons by which any human creature who tries to be truthful and faithful to duty can possibly be guided. Never abandon the wholesome practice of saying your own private prayers, night and morning. I have never abandoned it myself, and I know the comfort of it.

Try to do to others as you would have them do to you, and do not be discouraged if they fail sometimes. It is much better for you that they should fail in obeying the greatest rule laid down by our Saviour than that you should.

Your affectionate Father.

Very close to the original. Plorn sailed for New South Wales three weeks later; his father wept on the platform. They never met again — Dickens died twenty-one months after. Whatever the novels' quarrels with churches and their pew-rents, this letter is where his actual creed is kept.

Office of All the Year Round — to a Reader who has Written In About the Death of a Favourite Character
Dear Madam,

You reproach me with cruelty, and you are not the first; the mail upon this subject arrives by the sackful, and I read it, I assure you, with the greatest satisfaction. But consider: had the character lived, you would have forgotten her by Michaelmas. As she died, you have written me four pages in a hand shaking with indignation, and you will remember her all your life. Which of us, then, has used her better?

I am asked why I kill what my readers love. Madam, I kill what I love. When Paul Dombey died I walked the streets of Paris half the night; when I finished the storm at Yarmouth I could not speak. If it costs you nothing, it is not a story; it is an advertisement.

Faithfully yours (and grieving with you),
Charles Dickens

A composite, in the spirit of hundreds he actually sent — he did answer them, and he did walk Paris in mourning for Paul Dombey, a character he had himself resolved to kill five numbers earlier.

Habits of the correspondent

Blue ink, a goose-quill, and a slanting hand that accelerated dangerously toward the signature. Letters written before breakfast, business before noon, fiction till two, then the walk — twelve miles, at pace, composing aloud and alarming pedestrians. He kept his desk-ornaments in fixed positions (the duelling frogs, the rabbit on a gilt leaf) and could not write if they were moved, in whatever hotel on whatever continent.

How to address him

Friends: My dear Dickens. Family: The Inimitable (his own coinage, deployed without detectable irony), or the Sparkler of Albion. To his children he was also, by his own account, the Venerable. Persons wishing to reach him today are directed to the Circumlocution Office, where their enquiry will receive the fullest possible attention of its kind.

Gad's Hill Place, Higham by Rochester, Kent
