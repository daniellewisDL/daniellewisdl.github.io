The rest of the shelf

He never stopped being a journalist. Before Boz there was a shorthand reporter who could take down a parliamentary debate at speed in a jolting coach by lantern-light — and everything after, from the Christmas Books to the last essay, kept the reporter's habit: go, look, then make them look too.

The Five Christmas Books, 1843–1848

The man who invented Christmas (retail division)

Not invented, strictly — but rescued, rebranded, and shipped. Before 1843, Christmas was a fading rural feast; after the Carol, it was a moral occasion with a menu. When he died, a barrow-girl in Drury Lane is said to have asked: "Dickens dead? Then will Father Christmas die too?" The management keeps her question on file as its best-ever notice.

1843

A Christmas Carol

In prose · being a ghost story of Christmas

"Marley was dead: to begin with."

Six weeks, October to December, walking London fifteen and twenty miles a night while he wrote it, "laughing and weeping as he went." He insisted on a handsome binding at a low price and made almost nothing on six thousand copies sold by Christmas Eve. The pirates made plenty; he sued, won, and was left paying his own costs when they declared bankruptcy — a lesson filed under Copyright. The book asks Scrooge's own question back at him, and at you, annually, forever.

Ignorance & Want
1844

The Chimes

A goblin story — and the angriest of the five

Written in Genoa, homesick for London bells. Trotty Veck, a ticket-porter, is talked by newspapers and aldermen into believing the poor are "born bad." The goblins of the bells show him where that doctrine leads. The author read it aloud to friends before publication and reported with satisfaction that he had "undoubtedly made them cry." Alderman Cute, who intends to Put Suicide Down among the starving, was recognised in certain quarters. He was meant to be.

Contempt for the poor
1845

The Cricket on the Hearth

A fairy tale of home

The gentlest of the set — a carrier, his young wife, a mysterious lodger, and a cricket that chirps as guardian spirit of the hearth. Outsold the Carol on first issue, was adapted for seventeen London stages more or less at once, and remains the one to read when you have had enough of the world rather than enough of injustice.

Hearth & home
1846

The Battle of Life

A love story

The only one with no ghost, goblin, or cricket in it, and the one its author sweated hardest over for the least thanks. A tale of sisterly sacrifice on an old battlefield turned orchard. The management concedes the public's verdict but notes the public also bought 23,000 copies on the first day, so the public may inspect its own conscience.

Sacrifice
1848

The Haunted Man

The ghost's bargain

A chemist is granted the gift of forgetting all sorrow and wrong — and finds that with the memory goes the mercy. The last and darkest Christmas Book, with its lesson carried by the wildest of his street children: a boy so neglected he is described as not a child but "a growth of the streets." Lord, keep my memory green.

Memory & mercy
"This boy is Ignorance. This girl is Want. Beware them both… but most of all beware this boy, for on his brow I see that written which is Doom, unless the writing be erased." — The Ghost of Christmas Present, delivering the sharpest sentence of 1843

The Christmas Numbers, 1850–1867

Eighteen Christmases, conducted

Every December, Household Words and then All the Year Round issued a special Christmas number — framed tales with contributors (Collins, Gaskell, Mrs Linton) writing to the Conductor's design, and the Conductor rewriting them when they didn't. Highlights of the frame-stories he kept for himself:

1854

The Seven Poor Travellers

Richard Watts's Charity at Rochester really exists — six poor travellers, one night's lodging, entertainment, and fourpence each, provided they be "not rogues or proctors." The seventh traveller is the narrator, who supplies the supper and the story.

1863

Mrs Lirriper's Lodgings

A Norfolk Street landlady with a heart the size of her house and sentences that arrive breathless, unpunctuated, and exactly right. The public adored her so thoroughly she was brought back the following Christmas by general demand.

1865

Doctor Marigold

A cheap-jack — a travelling salesman patter-artist — who adopts a deaf girl and learns her language. Contains the line about no one being useless who lightens the burdens of another, and the reading version left audiences wrecked from Edinburgh to Boston.

1866

The Signal-Man

A railway cutting, a spectre, a warning that arrives precisely too late. Written the year after the author himself crawled out of the Staplehurst wreck; the best ghost story of the century knows exactly what a railway can do to a body.

1857

The Perils of Certain English Prisoners

Written with Collins after the Indian Mutiny. Included here for completeness rather than pride; the management does not defend all its numbers equally, and this one least of all.

1868

George Silverman's Explanation

Late, strange, and quiet: a Preston cellar-child grown into a scrupulous, self-erasing clergyman, explaining a life spent apologising for existing. The critics took a century to notice it. They are still apologising, appropriately.

Where it began

Sketches by Boz (1836)

The first sketch was dropped "stealthily one evening at twilight, with fear and trembling, into a dark letter-box in a dark office up a dark court in Fleet Street." When it appeared in print he walked to Westminster Hall and stood there half an hour, his eyes "so dimmed with joy and pride that they could not bear the street."

The pen-name was family property: his young brother Augustus was nicknamed Moses, pronounced through the nose as Boses, shortened to Boz. Every gin-shop, pawnbroker, hackney stand, and parish beadle in these sketches is drawn from pavement level — the whole later canon in seed form, including the beadle.

The magazines

Conducted by Charles Dickens

Household Words (1850–59): fiction and campaigning journalism at three-halfpence weekly — sanitation, factory accidents, workhouse visits, Ragged Schools. Ended when he quarrelled with Bradbury & Evans, whereupon he did the Dickensian thing: killed the magazine and started an identical one.

All the Year Round (1859–70): opened with A Tale of Two Cities, rescued by Great Expectations, and carried The Uncommercial Traveller — the late night-walking essays ("Night Walks," "Shy Neighbourhoods") that are the finest London prose ever written by an insomniac, a field with distinguished competition.

The travels

1842

American Notes

The first American tour: mobbed like royalty, admired much of the republic, then published his findings — on slavery (a chapter of documented atrocity, quoted from the slaveowners' own runaway advertisements), on the press, on spitting. America's reviews were unfavourable. The chapter on slavery has aged rather better than the reviews.

1846

Pictures from Italy

A year in Genoa, Rome, and Naples, travelling by coach with the whole household. Contains the century's best account of a Vesuvius ascent by moonlight and its most reluctant account of an execution by guillotine — attended because he believed you must look at what is done in your name. See the Causes.

1851–53

A Child's History of England

History for his own children, conducted with total impartiality except toward Henry VIII ("a most intolerable ruffian, a disgrace to human nature, and a blot of blood and grease upon the History of England"), which the management stands behind unreservedly.

Gad's Hill Place, Higham by Rochester, Kent

"Whatever I have tried to do in life, I have tried with all my heart to do well; whatever I have devoted myself to, I have devoted myself to completely."
