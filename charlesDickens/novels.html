The Complete Novels · In Order of Appearance

Fifteen, in green wrappers

Issued in monthly numbers at a shilling, or weekly at three-halfpence — literature on the instalment plan, invented more or less by accident in 1836 and never bettered. Each part ended where you could least bear it to. New Yorkers stood on the docks shouting at the packet-boat for news of Little Nell. That was the idea.

1836–37 · 19 monthly numbers · Chapman & Hall

The Pickwick Papers

The one that invented him

"The first ray of light which illumines the gloom…"

Commissioned as captions to a sporting artist's plates; by number four the artist was dead, a Cockney bootblack named Sam Weller had walked in, and the captions were selling forty thousand copies a month. There were Pickwick hats, Pickwick cigars, Pickwick chintzes. The twenty-four-year-old author noted the lesson: the public could be reached monthly. Also contains the most consequential misdated note in the history of breach-of-promise law, and the Fleet Prison chapters where, mid-comedy, the shadow of his father's Marshalsea falls across the page for the first time.

Oliver Twist

The parish boy's progress

"Please, sir, I want some more."

The New Poor Law of 1834 had decided that workhouses should be made deliberately wretched, to discourage the poor from being poor. Two years later a nine-year-old asked for more gruel and the whole apparatus has never recovered. Beneath the melodrama of Fagin's kitchen sits the book's cold arithmetic: a child is worth five pounds to whoever will take him, and the parish board — gentlemen of wisdom and philosophy, every one — cannot understand why he is not grateful.

Nicholas Nickleby

The Yorkshire schools, exposed

"There once lived, in a sequestered part of the county of Devonshire…"

Before writing it, the author travelled to Yorkshire under a false name to inspect the cheap boarding schools where unwanted boys were warehoused, starved, and forgotten — no holidays, no questions. Wackford Squeers of Dotheboys Hall ("Ex-perience does it") was recognisably assembled from life, and within a decade most of the real schools had closed for want of pupils. Fiction as demolition. Also contains the Crummles theatrical troupe and the Infant Phenomenon, for balance.

The Old Curiosity Shop

The death that stopped two continents

"Night is generally my time for walking."

Little Nell and her gambling-ruined grandfather flee London on foot through an industrialising England. Her death was the great shared grief of the age — Daniel O'Connell threw the book out of a train window; crowds at New York piers called to incoming ships, "Is Little Nell dead?" Posterity has smirked (Wilde: one must have a heart of stone to read it without laughing), but posterity did not lose a child in three by the age of five, and the Victorians did. Also contains Quilp, the most cheerfully malevolent creature he ever made.

Barnaby Rudge

A tale of the riots of 'eighty

"In the year 1775, there stood upon the borders of Epping Forest…"

His first historical novel: the anti-Catholic Gordon Riots of 1780, written while Chartism smouldered outside the window — a study of how a mob is manufactured from the ignorant, the aggrieved, and the merely bored. Features Grip, a talking raven drawn from the author's own (which he had stuffed when it died of eating lead paint, then replaced, then had the replacement stuffed too). A certain Mr Poe of Philadelphia reviewed the book, admired the bird, and was heard from on the subject some four years later. Nevermore, indeed — Grip got there first.

Martin Chuzzlewit

Selfishness, anatomised — with an American detour

"As no lady or gentleman, with any claims to polite breeding…"

A whole family circling an inheritance like gulls over a fish-cart. Sales flagged, so the author packed his hero off to America — a swampy land-fraud called Eden — and settled certain scores from his 1842 tour, whereupon America was displeased and sales flagged anyway. Redeemed forever by Mr Pecksniff, architect (who never designed anything but other men's ideas), and Mrs Gamp, the gin-fortified nurse whose imaginary friend Mrs Harris supplies all her references. Every hired carer you have ever feared descends from her — which is why he campaigned for trained nursing ever after.

1846–48 · 20 monthly numbers

Dombey and Son

Dealings with the firm — wholesale, retail, and for exportation

"Dombey sat in the corner of the darkened room…"

A merchant prince who wants a son for the firm's letterhead and gets, to his lifelong bafflement, a daughter worth ten of him. The first novel he planned in full before writing — the maturity starts here. The railway tears through Staggs's Gardens like the new age itself, and is given the book's most terrible piece of business to do. What is a child for? Mr Dombey's answer is the letterhead. The book spends nine hundred pages correcting him.

1849–50 · 20 monthly numbers

David Copperfield

The personal history — and his favourite child

"Whether I shall turn out to be the hero of my own life, or whether that station will be held by anybody else, these pages must show."

"Of all my books, I like this the best." The wine-bottling warehouse where young David labours is Warren's Blacking with the label changed; Mr Micawber, waiting magnificently for something to turn up, is John Dickens with the affection left in and the shame taken out. Note the initials: D.C., C.D. — he denied for years that it meant anything, convincing no one. Contains Peggotty, Betsey Trotwood, the Heep ("I am well aware that I am the 'umblest person going"), and the storm chapter at Yarmouth, which he could never read aloud without the room going silent as a church.

1852–53 · 20 monthly numbers

Bleak House

The masterpiece, by common consent of everyone but its author

"London. Michaelmas term lately over… Fog everywhere."

Jarndyce and Jarndyce drones on in Chancery until the entire estate is consumed in costs — based on real suits, one of which had then been running since 1798, and litigants in the gallery recognised themselves. Twin narrators, a detective (Inspector Bucket, the first in English fiction worth the name), a case of spontaneous combustion the author defended as documented science against all comers, and Jo the crossing-sweeper, who knows nothing, is moved on by everyone, and dies of the neglect of all. The chapter where he does so ends by addressing the reader directly: "And dying thus around us every day." The fog is the system. The system is the fog.

1854 · Household Words, weekly

Hard Times

For these times

"Now, what I want is, Facts. Teach these boys and girls nothing but Facts."

His shortest novel and his angriest: Coketown, a mill-town of machinery and tall chimneys where fancy has been outlawed and children are numbered like inventory. Mr Gradgrind's school teaches Facts until his own children break on them; Mr Bounderby, self-made man, turns out to have manufactured even the self. A book-length answer to the political economists who could price a man's labour to the farthing and could not say what he was for. Dedicated to Thomas Carlyle, who agreed with every word.

1855–57 · 20 monthly numbers

Little Dorrit

The Marshalsea, at last, by name

"Thirty years ago, Marseilles lay burning in the sun, one day."

A child born and raised inside the Marshalsea debtors' prison — the prison that held the author's own father, a fact he told almost no one living. Around her: Mr Dorrit, the Father of the Marshalsea, genteel to the last farthing of other people's money; the swindler-financier Merdle, before whom all society kneels until the bath-house morning it discovers what he was; and the Circumlocution Office, the whole science of government reduced to one principle — HOW NOT TO DO IT. The prisons in this book are only half of them buildings. Its author considered it among his best. Posterity, catching up slowly, now agrees.

1859 · All the Year Round, weekly

A Tale of Two Cities

The best-selling novel ever written, by some counts

"It was the best of times, it was the worst of times…"

London and Paris, the Terror, a wine-cask broken open on cobblestones and the stain that will not scrub out. A man recalled to life after eighteen years in the Bastille; a wastrel barrister who keeps his promise the only way left to him; and Madame Defarge, knitting the names of the condemned into her register. The book's warning is not that revolutions are wicked — it is that they are grown, sown by aristocracies who "sow the same seed of rapacious licence and oppression over again." A far, far better thing at the end of it than fiction had ever done.

1860–61 · All the Year Round, weekly

Great Expectations

Written to save the magazine; saved rather more

"My father's family name being Pirrip, and my Christian name Philip, my infant tongue could make of both names nothing longer or more explicit than Pip."

A boy is kind to a convict in a churchyard and spends the rest of the book being punished by the arrival of everything he wished for. Miss Havisham in her stopped-clock bridal ruin; Joe Gargery at the forge, the truest gentleman in the canon and the one Pip is ashamed of; and the question the whole late Dickens turns on — where does money come from, and what does it make of the people it lands on? The author wrote two endings. He published the merciful one. Readers have conducted the appeal ever since, in chambers, without result.

1864–65 · 20 monthly numbers

Our Mutual Friend

The last completed novel

"In these times of ours, though concerning the exact year there is no need to be precise…"

A fortune founded literally on dust-heaps — the great refuse mounds of London, worth their weight in gold — and a corpse fished from the Thames that turns out to be the wrong man, or possibly the right one. Society dines at the Veneerings', bran-new people with bran-new friends; Bella Wilfer is bought, and objects; Betty Higden flees across country rather than die in the workhouse, and her flight is the fiercest anti-Poor-Law writing he ever set down — see the postscript, where he drops the novelist's mask entirely. Written after the Staplehurst crash, with the manuscript he climbed back into the wreckage to save.

The Mystery of Edwin Drood

Unfinished. Permanently. Do not write in.

"An ancient English Cathedral tower? How can the ancient English Cathedral tower be here!"

Cloisterham, thinly Rochester; John Jasper, choirmaster and opium-eater, devoted to his nephew Edwin in a manner that makes the reader check the locks; and Edwin himself, vanished on Christmas Eve. The author died at Gad's Hill on 9 June 1870, the day after writing his last page of it, and took the solution with him. Half the pleasure since has been the industry of completion — séances, sequels, mock trials with Chesterton presiding and Shaw on the jury. The management's position remains: Jasper did it. The management notes it has been wrong about the management before.
