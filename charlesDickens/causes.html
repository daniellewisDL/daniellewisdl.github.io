The Agitation · Conducted Without Interruption Since 1836

The causes, and the questions

He distrusted Parliament (he had reported it verbatim for four years, which will do it), never stood for office though safe seats were offered, and joined few societies. His method was different: make the comfortable reader see one child, one prisoner, one fever-court — then ask, in front of everyone, how it got there. Each cause below ends with his question. Check for yourself which ones have closed.

I

The Workhouse & the New Poor Law

The Poor Law Amendment Act of 1834 made relief conditional on entering the workhouse, and made the workhouse deliberately worse than the worst life outside it — families separated, diet calculated to the ounce, the whole engine designed on the theory that poverty is a moral failing to be deterred. He opened fire in Oliver Twist two years later and never ceased: Betty Higden in his last completed novel would still rather die in a ditch than enter one. In the postscript to Our Mutual Friend he stepped out from behind the fiction entirely: "I believe there has been in England, since the days of the STUARTS, no law so often infamously administered."

"Is it lawful to make destitution a punishment — and then to be surprised by what the punished become?"

II

Child Labour & the Stolen Childhood

He knew this one from inside: at twelve, ten-hour days pasting labels at Warren's Blacking while the family lodged in the Marshalsea. In 1843 he read the Second Report on Children's Employment — children of seven dragging coal-tubs on all fours — and promised a pamphlet, "An Appeal to the People of England on behalf of the Poor Man's Child." He then reconsidered the weapon: a pamphlet reaches hundreds. By Christmas he had delivered instead "a sledge-hammer blow… with twenty times the force" — A Christmas Carol, with Ignorance and Want under the Spirit's robe. The pamphlet is forgotten because it was never written. The sledge-hammer is read every December.

"What does a nation expect to harvest, that sows its children into its mines and counting-houses?"

III

Ragged Schools & the Right to Learn

In 1843 he visited the Field Lane Ragged School, Saffron Hill — Fagin's own district — where volunteers taught the children no other school would admit, in rooms he described as foul beyond any decent person's imagining. He recruited Miss Burdett-Coutts's money for washing facilities before books ("you cannot teach a child who is tormented by vermin"), publicised the schools for years, and simultaneously savaged the opposite failure: education as fact-cramming machinery. Gradgrind's school in Hard Times and Dotheboys Hall in Nickleby are the two ditches on either side of the road he wanted built.

"Beware the boy, for on his brow is written Doom, unless the writing be erased. Who holds the eraser, if not you?"

IV

Sanitation & the Fever Courts

Of all the causes, the one he called first: "Searching Sanitary Reform must precede all other social remedies," he told the Metropolitan Sanitary Association in 1851 — even education, even temperance, "for neither can a schoolmaster nor a clergyman compete with a pestilential atmosphere." Tom-all-Alone's in Bleak House is the argument as architecture: the slum the great world ignores, whose fever nonetheless walks uphill and into the mansion, because contagion is the one thing in England that observes no class distinctions. Cholera made his point three times in his lifetime; Bazalgette's sewers finally conceded it.

"His Reverence Tom is not to be spoken to — but Tom has his revenge, and its name is contagion. Did you suppose the air asked for references?"

V

Debtors' Prisons & the Separate System

The Marshalsea took his father in 1824 for a baker's bill of forty pounds; the son took the Marshalsea in return, in Pickwick, in Copperfield, and finally by name in Little Dorrit, by which time the original had closed and he walked its surviving yard to check the geography. On prisons for criminals he was no sentimentalist — but the fashionable "separate system" of total solitary confinement, which he inspected at Philadelphia in 1842, he condemned in terms a modern report could reprint unedited: "a secret punishment which slumbering humanity is not roused up to stay… this slow and daily tampering with the mysteries of the brain is immeasurably worse than any torture of the body."

"We lock a man away for owing money, whereby he can earn none. Was the arithmetic ever meant to work — or was the working never the point?"

VI

Public Executions

He attended so that his testimony could not be answered with "you were not there": Courvoisier's hanging in 1840, the guillotine at Rome in 1845, and the Mannings at Horsemonger Lane in 1849, where thirty thousand came as to a fair. His letters to the Times that November made the respectable classes look at the crowd their law was convening. Public execution ended in Britain in 1868 — two years before his death, one of the few files on this page he saw closed himself.

"When the law kills as a public entertainment, inspect the audience it has assembled — then say again whom the lesson was for, and what it taught."

VII

Urania Cottage — the quiet one

With Angela Burdett-Coutts's fortune and his own management, a home in Shepherd's Bush (opened 1847) for women leaving prostitution and prison — run on cheerfulness, music, decent dresses, a savings-book, and no sermons about the past. He interviewed candidates in gaol himself, kept a private case-book of every woman's history "taken from her own lips," chose the furniture, and audited the matron. Roughly a hundred women emigrated to new lives. He ran it for over ten years and never published a word about it. The man who publicised everything kept his best deed anonymous — which is either the punchline or the point, and he would say both.

"Not 'What have you been?' but 'What do you mean to be?' — try the exchange of questions on anyone society has finished judging, and see what alters."

VIII

International Copyright (a personal matter, freely admitted)

American publishers reprinted his complete works for decades without paying him a farthing — perfectly legally, there being no international copyright. He said so on his 1842 tour, at dinners held in his honour, and was informed by the American press that it was indelicate of the guest to mention the theft at the banquet. The Carol, priced at five shillings in London, sold in America for six cents. He returned in 1867–68, was received with open arms and open cash-boxes for the readings, and graciously called for bygones. The bygones, it should be noted, had by then paid for Gad's Hill twice over. An international copyright law arrived in America in 1891, twenty-one years too late to reach him.

"Is the labourer worthy of his hire, or does the principle stop at the water's edge? (Asked at your own banquet, in your own honour, to your own publishers — try it.)"

IX

The Circumlocution Office — government by deferral

Behind the joke stood the Crimean winter of 1854–55, when British soldiers froze and sickened to death within reach of supplies that could not be issued for want of the correct form. He joined the Administrative Reform Association, and then built the permanent monument: the Office in Little Dorrit "beforehand with all the public departments in the art of perceiving HOW NOT TO DO IT," staffed hereditarily by Barnacles, where the inventor Doyce is treated as a public offender for having an idea. Every citizen since who has been transferred to the department that no longer exists has stood in Doyce's queue. You may experience the original here.

"When a public office is examined, ask not what it does — ask who is comfortable so long as it does nothing."

The Open Files

A note for the modern reader

The workhouse is closed; the questions it answered badly are not. Every age builds its own Circumlocution Office, its own Tom-all-Alone's just out of sight of the good streets, its own boards of gentlemen astonished that the boy should ask for more. Dickens's method remains available and requires no committee: go and look; find the one child in the statistic; then ask the question loudly, in company, at the moment it is least convenient. He was sentimental, theatrical, occasionally unjust, frequently impossible — and he moved more actual furniture in the actual world than any English novelist before or since. The files above are marked open on purpose. They are the inheritance.
