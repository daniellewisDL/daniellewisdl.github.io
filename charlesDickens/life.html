1812 – 1870 · Portsmouth to Poets' Corner

The Life of the Inimitable

"Whether I shall turn out to be the hero of my own life, or whether that station will be held by anybody else, these pages must show." The station, on the evidence, was held by a supporting cast of roughly nine hundred and eighty-nine.

1812

Born at Landport, Portsmouth, 7th February

Second of eight children of John Dickens, a Navy Pay Office clerk with a gift for hospitality and none whatever for arithmetic, and Elizabeth, who could tell a story and never quite forgave being put into several.

1817–22

Chatham — the good years

Kent childhood: books in the attic (Fielding, Smollett, the Arabian Nights, read "as if for life"), and walks with his father past a mansion called Gad's Hill Place, where the boy was told that if he were to be very persevering and work very hard, he might some day come to live in it. File that away; the boy did.

1824

The Marshalsea, and Warren's Blacking

John Dickens imprisoned for debt; Charles, twelve, sent to paste labels on bottles of boot-blacking at six shillings a week, in a rotten riverside warehouse, in a window where passers-by could watch him work. The family joined the prison; he lodged alone. It lasted about a year. He told two people in his lifetime and every reader since, in code — the code being roughly half the novels. When the family finances recovered, his mother was for sending him back to the warehouse: "I never afterwards forgot," he wrote. "I never shall forget, I never can forget."

1827–34

Solicitor's clerk · shorthand reporter · "Boz"

Taught himself Gurney's brachygraphy — "that savage stenographic mystery" — and became the fastest parliamentary reporter in the gallery, then a flying election correspondent for the Morning Chronicle, writing on his knees in post-chaises by lantern light. Four years of listening to Parliament verbatim left him with views on Parliament (see Causes, all of them). First sketch published 1833; he was twenty-one.

1836–37

Pickwick, marriage, and the arrival of everything at once

Marries Catherine Hogarth; publishes Sketches by Boz; and by the fourth number of Pickwick becomes, at twenty-four, the most read author in England, a position he then held for thirty-four years without once relaxing his grip or his schedule. Also 1837: the death of his sister-in-law Mary Hogarth, seventeen, in his arms — the only time in his career he ever missed a serial deadline, and the grief behind a decade of doomed heroines.

1842

America, first attempt

Received like visiting royalty — crowds, balls, a "Boz Ball" with three thousand guests — until he mentioned international copyright aloud, twice, at dinners given in his honour, and discovered what the American press thought of ungrateful guests. Went home and published American Notes anyway. Relations cooled for twenty-five years; see 1867 for the reconciliation, with box-office figures.

1843–48

The Christmas Books · Switzerland · Paris

The Carol in six weeks of night-walking; then restless years abroad — Genoa, Lausanne, Paris — dragging the growing household (ten children eventually, names including Alfred D'Orsay Tennyson Dickens and Edward Bulwer Lytton Dickens; he named children the way he named characters, irrevocably). Complained in Switzerland that he could not write without London streets to walk: "the toil and labour of writing, day after day, without that magic lantern, is IMMENSE."

1850

Household Words · Copperfield · the summit

Launches his weekly magazine and finishes his favourite novel in the same year, while running Urania Cottage, a family, an amateur theatrical company that performed before the Queen, and a correspondence the size of a government department's. Sleep: attained facing north, when attained. The bed was realigned with a compass to ensure it.

1856–58

Gad's Hill purchased · the marriage ends · the readings begin

Buys the very house on the Rochester road his father had pointed out to him at nine — the completed circle he told everyone about, because it was the respectable half of the story. Then the unrespectable half: separation from Catherine after twenty-two years and ten children, conducted with a public self-justification (published, catastrophically, in his own magazine) that remains the least defensible document he ever signed; and the beginning of his attachment to the young actress Ellen Ternan, kept so thoroughly from view that biography only assembled it a century later. In the same breath he began the paid public readings — the author as performer, nightly, to thousands.

1865

Staplehurst, 9th June

Returning from France with Ellen Ternan, his train jumped a gap in repaired track; the front carriages went off the viaduct. He clambered out, tended the dying at the bottom of the bank with a hat full of water and a flask of brandy — then climbed back into the hanging carriage to retrieve the manuscript of the next number of Our Mutual Friend. He was never comfortable on a train again, and wrote "The Signal-Man" the next year. He died five years later to the day.

1867–68

America, second attempt — all forgiven, all sold out

Seventy-six readings; queues of a mile in Boston frost; scalpers, a private secretary, and a profit of some £19,000, at severe cost to a failing body. At the farewell dinner he promised to record, in every future edition of American Notes, how generously the country had received him. He kept the promise. He also kept the £19,000.

1868–70

The farewell readings · "Sikes and Nancy"

Against every medical instruction, he added the murder of Nancy to the repertoire and performed it with such violence that ladies fainted, his pulse had to be recorded like a boxing second's chart, and his son begged him to stop. "I shall tear myself to pieces," he told a friend — accurately, and by way of explaining why he would continue. Final reading, St James's Hall, 15th March 1870: "from these garish lights I vanish now for evermore, with a heartfelt, grateful, respectful, and affectionate farewell."

1870

9th June — Gad's Hill

A full day's work on Edwin Drood in the Swiss chalet across the road (a present from an actor friend, shipped flat in fifty-eight boxes); collapse at dinner; died the next evening without regaining consciousness, five years to the day after Staplehurst. He asked for a quiet burial at Rochester "in an inexpensive, unostentatious, and strictly private manner." The nation, in the last of their long collaborations, overruled him: Poets' Corner, Westminster Abbey, the grave left open two days while thousands filed past and dropped in flowers. Luke Fildes drew the study with its empty chair, and Drury Lane asked whether Father Christmas would die too.
