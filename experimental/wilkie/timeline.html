Timeline & Gallery

A chronological journey through the life, works, and times of Wilkie Collins

The Life in Years

Major milestones from birth to legacy

1824

Birth

William Wilkie Collins is born on 8 January at 11 New Cavendish Street, Marylebone, London. His father is William Collins RA, a prosperous landscape painter; his mother Harriet Geddes. He is named after his godfather, the painter Sir David Wilkie.

1828

A Brother Arrives

Charles Allston Collins is born — Wilkie's younger brother, who will become a Pre-Raphaelite painter and later marry Kate Dickens.

1836–1838

Italian Sojourn

The Collins family travels to Italy, spending extended periods in Rome, Naples, and Sorrento. The twelve-year-old Wilkie is enchanted by Italian life and writes his first story. The experience profoundly shapes his imagination and love of narrative.

1841

Apprenticed in Commerce

Aged seventeen, Collins is apprenticed to Antrobus & Company, tea importers in the Strand. He finds the work crushingly dull but absorbs the bustling life of commercial London.

1846

Enters Lincoln's Inn

Collins begins studying law at Lincoln's Inn. He will be called to the bar in 1851 but never practise. The legal training proves invaluable for the intricate plots of his novels.

1847

Death of Father

William Collins dies. Wilkie writes his biography, Memoirs of the Life of William Collins, Esq., R.A. (published 1848) — his first published work.

1850

First Novel

Antonina; or, the Fall of Rome published — an ambitious historical romance set during the Gothic siege of Rome. It receives favourable reviews and sells well.

1851

Meeting Dickens

Collins takes a minor role in a production of Bulwer-Lytton's Not So Bad As We Seem, directed by Dickens. The two men meet and form an instant bond that will shape both their lives and careers.

1852

Basil Published

Collins's daring second novel — a modern tale of sexual obsession and betrayal — shocks critics and catches Dickens's enthusiastic attention. Collins becomes a regular contributor to Household Words.

1856

The Frozen Deep

Collins writes the melodrama The Frozen Deep, inspired by the lost Franklin Expedition. Dickens stars as Richard Wardour with electrifying intensity. The play's later performances in Manchester bring Dickens into contact with Ellen Ternan.

1857

The Dead Secret & Travels

Collins publishes The Dead Secret and collaborates with Dickens on The Lazy Tour of Two Idle Apprentices. During their walking tour, Collins injures his leg on Carrick Fell — an accident that may have triggered his later gout.

c. 1858

Caroline Graves

Collins begins living with Caroline Graves and her daughter Harriet. The circumstances of their meeting remain mysterious — legend links it to the famous moonlit encounter with a woman in white.

1859–1860

⭐ The Woman in White

Serialised in All the Year Round to sensational success. The novel creates a cultural phenomenon and establishes Collins as one of the most popular and innovative novelists of the day. The "sensation novel" is born.

1862–1863

No Name

Collins's third masterpiece — the story of Magdalen Vanstone, one of fiction's most audacious heroines, who fights to reclaim her inheritance after being disinherited by a cruel quirk of law.

1864–1866

Armadale

An intricate, dreamlike novel featuring the unforgettable villainess Lydia Gwilt. The novel's exploration of fate, free will, and prophetic dreams marks a high point of Collins's ambition.

1868

⭐ The Moonstone

Published serially in All the Year Round. Often called the first detective novel in English, it introduces Sergeant Cuff, Gabriel Betteredge, and a mystery involving a sacred Indian diamond. T.S. Eliot will later call it "the first, the longest, and the best of modern English detective novels."

1868

Personal Upheaval

Caroline Graves briefly leaves Collins to marry Joseph Clow. Collins begins his relationship with Martha Rudd, who will become the mother of his three children. Harriet Collins (his mother) dies.

1869

First Child

Marian (named after Marian Halcombe of The Woman in White) is born to Collins and Martha Rudd, registered under the surname "Dawson." Caroline Graves returns to Collins.

1870

Death of Dickens

Charles Dickens dies on 9 June, aged 58. Collins is devastated. He publishes Man and Wife, an attack on the marriage laws, but the loss of his greatest friend and champion marks a turning point.

1871–1874

Two More Children

Harriet Constance (1871) and William Charles (1874) are born to Collins and Martha Rudd. Collins maintains two households — one with Caroline, one with Martha — for the rest of his life.

1873

The New Magdalen

Both novel and play, The New Magdalen champions a "fallen woman" seeking redemption. The theatrical production is a major success. Collins's brother Charles dies, aged only 45.

1875

The Law and the Lady

Features one of fiction's first female detectives — Valeria Woodville — who investigates the "Not Proven" verdict in her husband's murder trial. A landmark for women in detective fiction.

1883

Heart and Science

Collins publishes a crusading anti-vivisection novel, reflecting his lifelong compassion for animals. His health continues to decline, but his literary output remains prodigious.

1889

Death

Wilkie Collins dies on 23 September at 82 Wimpole Street, London, aged 65. He is buried at Kensal Green Cemetery. His gravestone reads simply: "Author of The Woman in White and other works of fiction."

A Gallery of Words

Memorable passages from the Collins canon

"This is the story of what a Woman's patience can endure, and what a Man's resolution can achieve." — The Woman in White
"When you have found Abel Dodd, you have found the finest cricket-ball player known to the present age. He is absolutely the first slow bowler of his time." — Betteredge, The Moonstone
"I am (thank God) constitutionally superior to reason. No woman of true sensibility ever is." — Miss Clack, The Moonstone
"Any woman who is sure of her own wits is a match, at any time, for a man who is not sure of his own temper." — Marian Halcombe, The Woman in White
"Crimes cause their own detection, do they? And murder will out (another moral epigram), will it? Ask Coroners who sit at inquests in large towns if that is true, Lady Glyde." — Count Fosco, The Woman in White
"My mind is not a lodging-house, with rooms to let to anybody who may apply." — No Name

Recommended Reading

Essential works by and about Wilkie Collins for the curious reader

By Collins

  • The Woman in White (1860) — Start here. The masterpiece.
  • The Moonstone (1868) — The first detective novel.
  • No Name (1862) — His most radical heroine.
  • Armadale (1866) — His most ambitious plot.
  • Basil (1852) — The daring early masterpiece.
  • The Law and the Lady (1875) — First female detective.
  • Man and Wife (1870) — Social reform through fiction.

About Collins

  • Catherine Peters — The King of Inventors: A Life of Wilkie Collins (1991). The definitive biography.
  • William Clarke — The Secret Life of Wilkie Collins (1988). Focuses on his unconventional personal life.
  • Nuel Pharr Davis — The Life of Wilkie Collins (1956). Classic earlier biography.
  • Lillian Nayder — Unequal Partners: Charles Dickens, Wilkie Collins, and Victorian Authorship (2002). Essential for the Dickens relationship.
  • Jenny Bourne Taylor — In the Secret Theatre of Home (1988). Brilliant critical study.
