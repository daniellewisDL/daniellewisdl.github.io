Novels, Plays & Stories

The Literary Works

"Make 'em laugh, make 'em cry, make 'em wait."

A Canon of Innovation

Wilkie Collins published over thirty novels, numerous short stories, plays, and works of non-fiction across a career spanning four decades. His best work revolutionised English fiction.

The Woman in White

1859–1860 · Sensation Novel

Collins's masterpiece, serialised in Dickens's All the Year Round. A tale of conspiracy, false imprisonment, and stolen identity told through multiple narrators. The novel created a cultural phenomenon — spawning merchandise, stage adaptations, and the term "sensation novel" itself.

Key Characters: Walter Hartright, Marian Halcombe, Laura Fairlie, Count Fosco, Anne Catherick, Sir Percival Glyde

Themes: Identity, institutionalisation, women's legal status, the power of testimony

The Moonstone

1868 · Detective Novel

Widely regarded as the first detective novel in the English language. The theft of a sacred Indian diamond from an English country house sets in motion a mystery told through multiple narrators, featuring the immortal Sergeant Cuff and the eccentric Gabriel Betteredge.

Key Characters: Rachel Verinder, Franklin Blake, Sergeant Cuff, Gabriel Betteredge, Rosanna Spearman

Themes: Colonialism, opium and altered consciousness, class, the nature of evidence

No Name

1862–1863 · Sensation Novel

Two sisters discover after their parents' sudden deaths that they are illegitimate and disinherited. The resourceful Magdalen Vanstone embarks on an audacious campaign of disguise and deception to reclaim the family fortune — making her one of fiction's most complex anti-heroines.

Key Characters: Magdalen Vanstone, Norah Vanstone, Captain Wragge, Mrs. Lecount

Themes: Illegitimacy, inheritance law, female agency, identity and performance

Armadale

1864–1866 · Sensation Novel

Two young men — both named Allan Armadale — find their fates entangled by a murderous secret from the past. Enter Lydia Gwilt, one of the most mesmerising villainess-heroines in Victorian fiction: beautiful, brilliant, ruthless, and yet strangely sympathetic.

Key Characters: Allan Armadale, Ozias Midwinter, Lydia Gwilt, Dr. Downward

Themes: Fate vs. free will, dreams and prophecy, bigamy, redemption

Antonina; or, the Fall of Rome

1850 · Historical Romance

Collins's first novel — an ambitious historical epic set during the Gothic siege of Rome in 408 AD. Though heavily influenced by Bulwer-Lytton, it revealed Collins's instinct for dramatic plotting and atmospheric tension. The novel was commercially successful.

Themes: Faith vs. paganism, siege and survival, innocence corrupted

Basil

1852 · Sensation Novel

A daring early masterpiece: a young gentleman of good family becomes infatuated with a linen-draper's daughter, leading to a secret marriage, betrayal, and catastrophe. The novel's frank treatment of sexual passion shocked reviewers but caught the eye of Charles Dickens.

Themes: Class transgression, obsession, betrayal, the dangers of secrecy

Hide and Seek

1854 · Novel

A mystery of parentage and identity centring on a deaf-mute young woman named Madonna, adopted by a kindly painter. Collins drew on his father's world of art for the setting and demonstrated a sensitive, progressive treatment of disability rare for the era.

Themes: Disability, adoption, the art world, hidden identities

The Dead Secret

1857 · Mystery Novel

A dying woman's confession triggers a quest to discover a terrible family secret concealed in the "Myrtle Room" of a crumbling Cornish manor. Collins's first true mystery novel, serialised in Household Words.

Themes: Class secrets, the weight of the past, female courage

Man and Wife

1870 · Social Problem Novel

A passionate attack on the confused state of marriage law in England, Scotland, and Ireland, and a prescient critique of the cult of athleticism. A woman finds herself legally married to a brutal man through an archaic Scottish custom.

Themes: Marriage law reform, toxic masculinity, women's rights, athletic obsession

The New Magdalen

1873 · Social Novel

Mercy Merrick, a "fallen woman" serving as a nurse in the Franco-Prussian War, seizes a chance to steal a dead woman's identity and respectable life. Collins's most sympathetic portrait of a woman society considers irredeemable.

Themes: Redemption, social hypocrisy, the double standard, Christian charity

The Law and the Lady

1875 · Detective/Sensation Novel

Valeria Woodville discovers her husband was tried for the murder of his first wife under Scottish law and received the ambiguous verdict "Not Proven." She resolves to prove his innocence — making her one of fiction's first female detectives.

Themes: Female detective agency, Scottish legal system, truth vs. proof

Heart and Science

1883 · Anti-Vivisection Novel

A polemical novel attacking the practice of vivisection, centring on the villainous Dr. Doyen. Though heavy-handed in its message, the novel evinces Collins's lifelong compassion for animals and his willingness to court controversy.

Themes: Animal rights, scientific ethics, the abuse of power

The Frozen Deep

1856 · Play (with Dickens)

A melodrama inspired by the lost Franklin Expedition to the Arctic. Dickens played the lead role of Richard Wardour with extraordinary passion — performances that would lead to his fateful meeting with Ellen Ternan. A pivotal moment in both men's lives.

Themes: Self-sacrifice, rivalry in love, extreme endurance

No Thoroughfare

1867 · Novella & Play (with Dickens)

A Christmas story serialised in All the Year Round and simultaneously staged as a play. A foundling raised by a London wine merchant discovers his true identity while journeying through the Swiss Alps. Collins and Dickens shared the writing.

Themes: Identity, forgery, Alpine adventure, the orphan's quest

The Lazy Tour of Two Idle Apprentices

1857 · Travel Narrative (with Dickens)

A fictionalised account of a walking tour of northern England undertaken by Collins and Dickens. The two authors wrote alternate chapters, creating a charming, witty record of their friendship and adventures — including Collins breaking his leg on Carrick Fell.

Themes: Friendship, travel, the English countryside, literary camaraderie

Collins's Narrative Art

The Multi-Narrator Technique

Collins's most distinctive innovation was his use of multiple first-person narrators, each contributing their portion of the story. In The Woman in White, he explicitly modelled this on legal testimony — each narrator swears to the truth of their account, and the reader becomes judge and jury.

This technique gave Collins unprecedented control over suspense (he could withhold information by switching narrators at critical moments), characterisation (each narrator's voice reveals their personality and biases), and dramatic irony (the reader often knows more than any individual narrator).

The Moonstone perfected the method, with its parade of wonderfully distinct voices — from the pious Gabriel Betteredge to the sanctimonious Miss Clack to the methodical Sergeant Cuff. No two accounts sound alike.

Women, Law & Social Conscience

Perhaps no Victorian male novelist was as consistently sympathetic to women as Wilkie Collins. His heroines are resourceful, intelligent, courageous, and morally complex. Marian Halcombe is physically brave and intellectually formidable; Magdalen Vanstone is audacious and unyielding; Valeria Woodville is a pioneering female detective.

Collins was acutely aware of the legal disabilities suffered by women under Victorian law. His novels explore the injustice of laws that stripped married women of property rights, allowed husbands near-absolute control over their wives, and punished "fallen women" while excusing the men who had seduced them.

His social novels of the 1870s and 1880s tackled these issues directly, earning him both admiration and hostility. As he wrote: "I have always believed in the future of women, and always maintained the rights of women."
