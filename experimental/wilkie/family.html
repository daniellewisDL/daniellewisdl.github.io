Love, Rebellion & Domesticity

Family & Personal Life

"I could not have lived as other men of my age and social standing lived. It was not in my nature."

An Unconventional Life

Wilkie Collins never married. In an age when marriage was all but compulsory for a man of his social standing, Collins deliberately chose a different path — maintaining two separate households, two partner-ships, and three children, none born in wedlock. It was a domestic arrangement that scandalised respectable Victorian society and fascinated posterity in equal measure.

To understand Collins's personal life is to understand the man himself — his fierce independence, his compassion for women whom society judged harshly, and his refusal to submit to conventions he found hypocritical. His fiction is suffused with these themes: the injustice of marriage law, the vulnerability of women without legal rights, the cruelty of social stigma.

The Collins Family

An artistic household that shaped the man and the writer

William Collins RA

Father (1788–1847)

A prominent Royal Academician known for his rustic landscapes and scenes of childhood. William Collins was devout, conventional, and socially ambitious — qualities that would set him at odds with his eldest son. Yet it was William who introduced Wilkie to the world of art and culture, filling the family home with painters, poets, and men of letters.

After his death in 1847, Wilkie honoured him with a two-volume biography — his first published work. The book reveals both filial love and a keen, unsentimental eye.

Harriet Geddes Collins

Mother (1790–1868)

Harriet Geddes came from a cultured but modest family. She was intelligent, warm, and supportive of her sons' creative ambitions. After William's death, she and Wilkie lived together for many years, and she remained a steadying presence in his life. Wilkie was devoted to her and deeply affected by her death in 1868.

Some biographers have seen in Collins's many caring, resourceful mother-figures a tribute to Harriet.

Charles Allston Collins

Brother (1828–1873)

Wilkie's younger brother was a gifted painter associated with the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood. His best-known painting, Convent Thoughts, was exhibited at the Royal Academy in 1851. Charles later married Kate Dickens, binding the Collins and Dickens families together. Plagued by chronic illness, he turned to writing and contributed to All the Year Round.

He designed the famous green cover for Dickens's unfinished The Mystery of Edwin Drood.

Caroline Graves

Companion & Partner (c. 1830–1895)

The most enduring and passionate relationship of Collins's life was with Caroline Elizabeth Graves (née Courtenay), a woman of obscure origins who became his companion, hostess, and — in all but legal name — his wife for nearly three decades.

According to a celebrated story (recounted by Millais's son), Collins first encountered Caroline one moonlit night in the 1850s, when a woman in white nightdress rushed out of a garden gate in Regent's Park, pale and distressed. This dramatic meeting — which may or may not be true — is often cited as the inspiration for the ghostly Anne Catherick in The Woman in White.

Caroline was a widow with a young daughter, Harriet (known as Carrie), when she began living with Collins around 1858. She presided over his household at various London addresses, acting as his hostess and housekeeper. Collins raised Carrie as his own.

In 1868, in a mysterious episode that has never been fully explained, Caroline briefly left Collins to marry Joseph Clow, a younger man. The separation was short-lived; Caroline soon returned to Collins and remained with him until his death. Whatever the reasons for her brief marriage — speculation includes a desire for the legal respectability Collins refused to offer — it left no lasting rift between them.

Martha Rudd

Second Partner & Mother of His Children (c. 1845–1919)

In 1868, the same year that Caroline temporarily left him, Collins began a second relationship with Martha Rudd, a young woman from Norfolk who worked as a domestic servant. Martha would become the mother of Collins's three children.

Collins maintained a second household for Martha and their children, who were registered under the surname "Dawson" — a name Collins used as an alias when visiting them. This arrangement continued in parallel with his relationship with Caroline for the rest of his life.

The children were:

  • Marian (born 1869) — named after Marian Halcombe of The Woman in White
  • Harriet Constance (born 1871)
  • William Charles (born 1874) — known as "Charley"

Collins provided generously for Martha and the children, both during his lifetime and in his will. He was by all accounts an affectionate, if unconventional, father. His decision to name his eldest daughter after his most celebrated fictional heroine suggests the depth of emotional investment he brought to his double life.

"He held what were then very advanced views on marriage, regarding it as a social institution that was often used to oppress women rather than protect them."

Life into Art

Collins's unconventional personal life was not merely a biographical curiosity — it was intimately connected to his art. His fiction is populated by women who suffer under the inequities of Victorian marriage law, who are denied property, security, and autonomy simply because they lack a husband or a wedding ring.

Magdalen Vanstone in No Name loses everything because her parents' belated marriage is invalidated by a technicality. The heroines of Man and Wife are trapped by the conflicting marriage laws of England, Scotland, and Ireland. The "new Magdalen" Mercy Merrick is condemned by society for a sexual "fall" that leaves the man unpunished.

Collins knew whereof he wrote. By maintaining two households outside the bonds of marriage, he lived the contradictions of his age: a prosperous, celebrated gentleman who chose to exist in a social no-man's-land, defying the very conventions his fiction so brilliantly anatomised.

"His was a life lived with remarkable courage, in defiance of the rigid social codes of his time, and always with compassion for those whom society condemned." — Catherine Peters, biographer
