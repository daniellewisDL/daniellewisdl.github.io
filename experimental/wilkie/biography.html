1824 – 1889

The Life of Wilkie Collins

"I have always held the old-fashioned opinion that the primary object of a work of fiction should be to tell a story."

Early Years & An Artistic Childhood

William Wilkie Collins was born on 8 January 1824 at 11 New Cavendish Street, Marylebone, London. He was the eldest son of William Collins, a celebrated Royal Academician known for his luminous landscape paintings, and Harriet Geddes, who came from a genteel but modest background. The name "Wilkie" honoured his godfather, the Scottish painter Sir David Wilkie.

From the very first, young Wilkie was immersed in a milieu of art, intellect, and creative ambition. The Collins household was a gathering place for painters, writers, and men of letters. Guests included such luminaries as the poet Samuel Taylor Coleridge, the painter William Etty, and the essayist Charles Lamb. The boy absorbed it all — the spirited conversations, the aroma of linseed oil, the romance of the creative life.

In 1826, the family was joined by a second son, Charles Allston Collins, named after the American painter Washington Allston. The two brothers would remain close throughout their lives, their fates intertwined with some of the greatest literary figures of the age.

"The world was full of pictures to my father; and those pictures, by a sort of gentle tyranny, filled the world for his children too."

Italy & the Awakening of Imagination

In 1836, when Wilkie was twelve, William Collins took his family on a Continental tour that would last nearly two years. They settled for long stretches in Rome, Naples, and Sorrento, and the experience proved transformative for the young Wilkie. Italy's dramatic landscapes, its vivid street life, its passion and colour, fired his imagination in ways that staid London never could.

It was in Italy that Collins wrote his first story — a tale composed to impress a schoolmate — and discovered a love of narrative that would never leave him. The sensory richness of Italy would pervade his later fiction, particularly in the atmospheric descriptions that became one of his signatures.

Upon returning to England in 1838, Wilkie was enrolled at a private school in Highbury, where he later claimed that a schoolyard bully taught him the art of storytelling: the older boy would demand bedtime tales as the price of leaving young Wilkie unharmed. "I owe my storytelling to that bully," Collins would later quip.

The Reluctant Lawyer

William Collins, ever the practical Victorian patriarch, was determined that at least one of his sons should pursue a respectable profession. In 1841, aged seventeen, Wilkie was apprenticed to the tea merchants Antrobus & Company in the Strand. The work bored him utterly, but it gave him access to the teeming life of London's commercial streets — material he would later mine in his fiction.

After five years of commerce, Collins pivoted to the law, entering Lincoln's Inn in 1846. He was called to the bar in 1851 but never practised. The legal training, however, proved invaluable: his novels are riddled with intricate legal plots, contested wills, questions of inheritance, and forensic argument — all rendered with an accuracy that delighted and alarmed the legal profession in equal measure.

"The law will tell you that the dying man's last will and testament is an instrument of the highest importance. The true importance is in his dying wishes." — from No Name

Literary Emergence

Collins's father died in February 1847, and Wilkie honoured his memory with a loving biography, Memoirs of the Life of William Collins, Esq., R.A. (1848). It was his first published book and revealed a graceful, observant prose style.

His first novel, Antonina; or, the Fall of Rome (1850), was an ambitious historical romance set during the siege of Rome by the Goths. Though derivative of Bulwer-Lytton, it showed Collins's gift for dramatic plotting and atmospheric tension. A second novel, Basil (1852), was far more daring — a tale of sexual obsession and betrayal set in contemporary London that shocked some readers and thrilled others.

It was Basil that brought Collins to the attention of Charles Dickens, beginning one of the most consequential literary friendships in English literature.

The Dickens Years: 1851–1870

The friendship between Collins and Dickens was immediate, deep, and remarkably productive. They met in 1851 when Collins took a small part in a performance of Bulwer-Lytton's play Not So Bad As We Seem, produced by Dickens. Within weeks they were inseparable, bonded by a shared love of theatre, a restless energy, and a taste for bohemian adventures.

Dickens became Collins's mentor, editor, and closest companion. Collins became a regular contributor to Dickens's journals Household Words and later All the Year Round. They travelled together, collaborated on Christmas stories, mounted amateur theatrical productions, and spurred each other to ever greater creative heights.

During this extraordinarily fertile period, Collins produced the works for which he is best remembered: The Dead Secret (1857), The Woman in White (1859–60), No Name (1862–63), Armadale (1864–66), and The Moonstone (1868).

"Make 'em laugh, make 'em cry, make 'em wait." — Wilkie Collins's famous summary of the art of fiction

The Sensation Novel

Collins did not merely write popular fiction; he invented a genre. The "sensation novel" — a thrilling blend of Gothic melodrama, realist social commentary, and detective intrigue set in contemporary England — was Collins's great innovation. The Woman in White was its prototype and greatest triumph.

Where earlier Gothic fiction placed its horrors in remote castles and foreign lands, Collins set his nightmares in comfortable English drawing rooms. The terrifying Count Fosco stalks the halls of an English country house; the white-robed phantom turns out to be a woman confined not in a dungeon but in a Victorian asylum. The horror is domestic, the conspiracy legal, the villain charming. It was revolutionary.

The critical establishment was scandalised. Highbrow journals denounced the sensation novel as vulgar, morbid, and morally corrosive. The public devoured every page. The Woman in White spawned perfumes, waltzes, bonnets, and even a brand of eau de Cologne. Nothing quite like it had been seen before.

Health, Laudanum & Later Years

From the mid-1850s onward, Collins suffered from increasingly severe rheumatic gout — a condition that caused agonising pain in his joints and eyes. To manage the torment, he turned to laudanum (a tincture of opium widely available without prescription in Victorian England). The drug provided relief but exacted a fearsome toll.

Collins's laudanum consumption grew to extraordinary levels; by the 1870s he was taking enough to kill several men unaccustomed to the drug. The opium fuelled vivid, often terrifying hallucinations. Collins claimed that a doppelgänger — a "ghost Wilkie" — would sit beside him and even attempt to take over his writing. This spectral double may have inspired some of his most unsettling fiction.

After Dickens's death in 1870, Collins's work became more overtly didactic, addressing social issues such as the rights of fallen women, the injustice of the marriage laws, and religious hypocrisy. Novels like The New Magdalen (1873), The Law and the Lady (1875), and Heart and Science (1883) were passionate social tracts wrapped in narrative dress. Critics found them heavy-handed; modern readers often find them ahead of their time.

Final Years & Legacy

By the 1880s, Collins was physically frail but intellectually undiminished. He continued to write — often dictating to an amanuensis when his eyes failed him — and remained socially active, known for his warmth, his wit, and his prodigious hospitality.

Wilkie Collins died on 23 September 1889 at his home, 82 Wimpole Street, London. He was buried at Kensal Green Cemetery. His gravestone, at his own request, bears no elaborate epitaph — simply his name, dates, and the words "Author of The Woman in White and other works of fiction."

His literary reputation, which had dimmed in the decades after his death, was fully restored in the twentieth century. T. S. Eliot championed The Moonstone; Dorothy L. Sayers praised his narrative technique; and scholars recognised him as a pioneer of the detective genre, the thriller, and the multi-narrator novel. Today, Wilkie Collins stands secure as one of the great innovators of English fiction.

"He was a literary radical in the best sense — a man who reimagined what stories could do, who they could be about, and how they could be told." — A modern assessment
