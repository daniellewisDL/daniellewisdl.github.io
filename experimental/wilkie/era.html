The World That Made the Writer

The Victorian Era

"It was the age of machinery, the age of every conceivable kind of innovation — and yet, beneath it all, the old darkness persisted."

An Age of Contradictions

The reign of Queen Victoria (1837–1901) encompassed Wilkie Collins's entire adult life and career. It was an era of breathtaking transformation — industrial revolution, imperial expansion, scientific discovery, and social upheaval — but also one of profound anxiety, rigid moral codes, and stark inequality. Understanding the Victorian world is essential to understanding Collins, for his fiction was both a product of this world and a sustained, brilliant critique of it.

🏭 The Industrial Revolution

By the time Collins was born in 1824, Britain was the workshop of the world. Steam railways, textile mills, iron foundries, and coal mines had transformed the landscape and the economy. Millions had migrated from the countryside to burgeoning industrial cities — Manchester, Birmingham, Leeds — creating a new urban working class.

The fruits of industrialisation were unevenly distributed. The middle classes enjoyed unprecedented prosperity; factory workers endured sixteen-hour days, child labour, and squalid living conditions. This contrast — wealth and squalor side by side — animates Collins's social novels.

👑 Empire & Expansion

Victorian Britain commanded the largest empire in human history, spanning a quarter of the globe. The Imperial project permeated every aspect of Victorian life — from the exotic goods (tea, spices, diamonds) that stocked the shops, to the ideology of racial and cultural superiority that justified colonial rule.

Collins engaged with Empire most directly in The Moonstone, whose plot turns on the theft of a sacred Indian diamond — an act that symbolises the larger pillage of colonial exploitation. The novel's Indian characters are treated with rare sympathy and dignity.

⚖️ Women & the Law

Under Victorian law, a married woman had virtually no independent legal existence. Upon marriage, her property, earnings, and even her children belonged to her husband. She could not sue, make contracts, or own property in her own name. Divorce was prohibitively expensive and socially ruinous for women.

Collins made the legal oppression of women a central theme of his fiction. The Woman in White exposes how easily a woman could be incarcerated by her husband; No Name anatomises the injustice of illegitimacy law; Man and Wife attacks the chaotic state of marriage legislation.

📰 The Rise of Serialisation

The Victorian era saw an explosion of popular literacy and a revolution in the way fiction was consumed. The serialised novel — published in weekly or monthly instalments in magazines — became the dominant literary form. Dickens's Household Words and All the Year Round were the most influential venues.

Collins was a master of serialisation. He crafted his novels with meticulous attention to the rhythm of weekly parts, ending each instalment on a cliffhanger that kept readers desperate for the next issue. The serialisation of The Woman in White caused queues outside the offices of All the Year Round.

🎭 The Sensation Novel

The "sensation novel" — a term coined by critics in the early 1860s — was the most controversial literary genre of its day. Combining elements of Gothic horror, domestic realism, and detective fiction, sensation novels set their thrills not in remote foreign settings but in the comfortable English home.

Collins, along with Mary Elizabeth Braddon (Lady Audley's Secret) and Mrs. Henry Wood (East Lynne), was a leading practitioner. Critics denounced the genre as morbid, immoral, and pandering to low taste. Readers loved it. The sensation novel's DNA survives in the modern thriller, the psychological suspense novel, and the domestic noir.

💊 Opium & Victorian Medicine

Laudanum — a tincture of opium dissolved in alcohol — was the painkiller of choice in Victorian England. Available without prescription from any chemist's shop, it was used by millions, from factory workers to aristocrats. De Quincey's Confessions of an English Opium-Eater (1821) had romanticised its use.

Collins became dependent on laudanum after it was prescribed for his rheumatic gout. His consumption eventually reached staggering quantities. Opium's influence on his work is unmistakable: the dream-sequences in Armadale, the drugged theft in The Moonstone, and the hallucinatory quality of his later prose all bear its mark.

The Literary Landscape

Collins's contemporaries and the rich world of Victorian letters

The Major Novelists

Collins wrote in an era when the English novel reached its zenith. His contemporaries included:

  • Charles Dickens — close friend and collaborator
  • William Makepeace Thackeray — satirist and social chronicler
  • George Eliot — the supreme psychological novelist
  • Anthony Trollope — prolific chronicler of political and clerical life
  • Charlotte & Emily Brontë — the wild geniuses of Yorkshire
  • Elizabeth Gaskell — social realist and biographer of Charlotte Brontë
  • Thomas Hardy — whose career began as Collins's was waning

Collins's relationship with this literary establishment was complex. He was hugely popular but never fully accepted by the critical elite, who regarded his work as too "sensational" and too commercial. His championing of plot over character placed him at odds with the dominant critical theory of his day.

Crime, Detection & the Law

The Victorian era saw the birth of the modern police force (the Metropolitan Police was founded in 1829) and, with it, a public fascination with crime, detection, and the criminal underworld. Collins was profoundly attuned to this fascination.

The Moonstone introduced the figure of the professional detective — Sergeant Cuff — drawing on the real-life detectives of Scotland Yard. Collins consulted actual police officers for accuracy and studied criminal cases with lawyerly precision.

His legal training at Lincoln's Inn gave him an expert's understanding of wills, contracts, marriage law, and courtroom procedure — knowledge he deployed with devastating effect in novel after novel. Few Victorian novelists understood the law as well as Collins, and none used it so creatively.

Class, Morality & Hypocrisy

Victorian society was rigidly stratified by class, and social respectability was its supreme currency. The middle-class ideal of the "Angel in the House" — the pure, submissive, domesticated woman — was both aspiration and prison. Men were expected to be sober, industrious, and godly (in public, at least). Those who transgressed — "fallen" women, unmarried mothers, sexual nonconformists — were cast into social exile.

Collins, who lived outside these norms by choice, saw through the hypocrisy with clear eyes. His fiction is populated by characters who are punished not for genuine wickedness but for violations of social convention. He reserved his fiercest contempt for the publicly virtuous who were privately corrupt — the Count Foscos and Sir Percival Glydes who wield respectability as a weapon.

At the same time, Collins celebrated those who live authentically — the Marian Halcombes, the Magdalen Vanstones, the Mercy Merricks — women who defy convention and claim agency over their own destinies. In this, Collins was decades ahead of his time.

"The great thing in life is not to be good, but to be kind. Goodness is a matter of convention; kindness is a matter of the heart." — attributed to Wilkie Collins
