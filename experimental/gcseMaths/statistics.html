6.1 Populations & Sampling

💡 Key Concept

A population is the entire group you want to study. A sample is a subset used to make inferences about the population.

Limitations of Sampling

  • A sample may not represent the full population.
  • Different samples give different results.
  • Sampling bias occurs when some members are more likely to be selected.
  • Larger samples are generally more reliable.

Types of Sampling

Random sampling: every member has an equal chance of selection.

Systematic sampling: select every kth item from a list.

Stratified sampling: divide population into groups (strata) and sample proportionally from each.

6.3 Histograms & Cumulative Frequency Higher

💡 Key Concept

A histogram looks like a bar chart but represents continuous data. The area of each bar represents frequency (not the height). For unequal class widths:

Frequency density = frequency ÷ class width

✏️ Worked Example — Drawing a Histogram

Given this grouped frequency table:

Plot with frequency density on the y-axis and time on the x-axis. The bar for 25–30 will be the tallest despite not having the highest frequency!

Cumulative Frequency

A cumulative frequency table adds up the frequencies as you go. Plot on a graph to find the median, lower quartile (LQ), upper quartile (UQ), and interquartile range (IQR).

Median = value at the n/2 th point

LQ = value at n/4 th point

UQ = value at 3n/4 th point

IQR = UQ − LQ

6.4 Averages & Spread

💡 Key Concept — Measures of Central Tendency

Mean = sum of all values ÷ number of values

Median = middle value when data is ordered

Mode = most frequently occurring value

Modal class = class interval with the highest frequency (for grouped data)

💡 Key Concept — Measures of Spread

Range = largest value − smallest value

IQR = UQ − LQ Higher

Outliers = values more than 1.5 × IQR beyond quartiles Higher

Box Plots Higher

A box plot shows the five-number summary: Minimum, LQ, Median, UQ, Maximum.

The box shows the IQR (middle 50% of data). Whiskers extend to min and max (or to the nearest non-outlier).

Box plots are excellent for comparing two data sets side by side.

Comparing Data Sets

When comparing, always state:

  1. A comparison of average (mean or median) — "on average, group A scored higher because…"
  2. A comparison of spread (range or IQR) — "group B was more consistent because the range was smaller…"
  3. In context — always refer to the original data context (e.g., test scores, heights)

6.5 Scatter Graphs & Correlation

💡 Key Concept

A scatter graph shows two variables plotted against each other. The pattern shows the correlation:

  • Positive correlation: as one increases, the other increases (upward trend)
  • Negative correlation: as one increases, the other decreases (downward trend)
  • No correlation: no clear pattern

⚠️ Correlation ≠ Causation

Just because two things are correlated does NOT mean one causes the other. For example, ice cream sales and drowning rates both increase in summer, but ice cream doesn't cause drowning!

Line of Best Fit

Draw a straight line that passes through the data with roughly equal numbers of points above and below. Use it to:

  • Interpolate: estimate values WITHIN the data range (reliable)
  • Extrapolate: estimate values OUTSIDE the data range (less reliable)
