5.1 Frequencies & Experiments

💡 Key Concept

Relative frequency = number of times an event occurs ÷ total number of trials. As trials increase, the relative frequency approaches the theoretical probability.

Recording Results

Use frequency tables and frequency trees to organise experimental data.

Fair and Biased

An experiment is fair if each outcome is equally likely. A coin is fair if P(heads) = P(tails) = 0.5.

If outcomes are NOT equally likely, the process is biased.

5.2 Probability Scales & Properties

💡 Key Concept

Probability is always between 0 (impossible) and 1 (certain). The probabilities of all mutually exclusive outcomes sum to 1.

P(not A) = 1 − P(A)
0 — Impossible 0.25 0.5 — Even chance 0.75 1 — Certain

Mutually Exclusive Events

Events that cannot happen at the same time. For mutually exclusive events A and B:

P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B)

✏️ Worked Example

A bag has 3 red, 5 blue, and 2 green balls. Find P(red) and P(not red).

Step 1: Total balls = 3 + 5 + 2 = 10
Step 2: P(red) = 3/10 = 0.3
Step 3: P(not red) = 1 − 0.3 = 0.7 (or 7/10) ✓

5.3 Sampling

💡 Key Concept

As the sample size increases, the experimental probabilities tend towards the theoretical probabilities. A larger, unbiased sample gives more reliable results.

Bias in Sampling

A sample is biased if it doesn't fairly represent the population. For example, surveying only morning shoppers to estimate total daily footfall.

A random sample gives every member of the population an equal chance of being selected.

✏️ Worked Example

In 200 spins of a spinner, red appeared 70 times. Estimate the probability of red.

Relative frequency = 70/200 = 7/20 = 0.35
So estimated P(red) ≈ 0.35 ✓

5.4 Possibility Spaces & Venn/Tree Diagrams

💡 Key Concept

A sample space lists all possible outcomes. Use tables, grids, Venn diagrams, or tree diagrams to systematically enumerate them.

Sample Space Diagrams

✏️ Worked Example

Two fair dice are rolled. Find P(total = 7).

Step 1: Total possible outcomes = 6 × 6 = 36
Step 2: Pairs summing to 7: (1,6), (2,5), (3,4), (4,3), (5,2), (6,1) → 6 ways
Step 3: P(total = 7) = 6/36 = 1/6 ✓

Venn Diagrams

Show the overlap between sets. The intersection (∩) shows items in BOTH sets. The union (∪) shows items in EITHER set.

P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A ∩ B)

✏️ Worked Example — Venn Diagram

In a class of 30: 18 play football, 12 play netball, 5 play both. Find P(plays neither).

Step 1: Only football = 18 − 5 = 13
Step 2: Only netball = 12 − 5 = 7
Step 3: Total playing at least one = 13 + 5 + 7 = 25
Step 4: Neither = 30 − 25 = 5. P(neither) = 5/30 = 1/6 ✓

Tree Diagrams

Branches show each possible outcome with its probability. Multiply along branches for AND, add between branches for OR.

✏️ Worked Example — Tree Diagram (without replacement)

A bag has 4 red and 6 blue balls. Two are picked without replacement. Find P(both red).

Step 1: P(1st red) = 4/10
Step 2: P(2nd red | 1st was red) = 3/9 (one fewer red, one fewer total)
Step 3: P(both red) = 4/10 × 3/9 = 12/90 = 2/15 ✓

5.5 Combined & Conditional Probability

💡 Key Concept

Independent events: one doesn't affect the other. P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)

Dependent events: one affects the other (e.g., picking without replacement).

Conditional Probability Higher

💡 Key Concept

Conditional probability is the probability of an event GIVEN that another has occurred:

P(A | B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B)

✏️ Worked Example — Two-Way Table

A survey asks 100 students about pets:

CatNo CatTotal
Dog152540
No Dog204060
Total3565100

Find: P(has a cat | has a dog)

Step 1: We're given "has a dog", so our total is 40 (dog row)
Step 2: Of those 40, 15 also have a cat
Step 3: P(cat | dog) = 15/40 = 3/8 ✓

Draw a tree diagram where the second set of branches changes based on the first outcome (for dependent events). Multiply along branches to find combined probabilities.

In a Venn diagram, conditional probability P(A|B) means: look only at circle B, and find what fraction is also in A.

P(A|B) = n(A ∩ B) / n(B)

Use a two-way table of expected frequencies. Given B has happened, restrict to B's row/column and find A within it.
