4.2 Constructions & Loci

💡 Key Concept

Constructions use only a ruler and compasses (no protractor). You must know how to construct:

  • Perpendicular bisector of a line segment
  • Angle bisector
  • Perpendicular from a point to a line
  • Perpendicular at a point on a line

Loci

A locus is a set of points satisfying a rule:

• Fixed distance from a point → circle

• Fixed distance from a line → parallel lines (or "sausage" shape)

• Equidistant from two points → perpendicular bisector

• Equidistant from two lines → angle bisector

4.3 Angle Facts

💡 Key Concept — Essential Angle Rules

• Angles on a straight line sum to 180°

• Angles at a point sum to 360°

• Vertically opposite angles are equal

• Interior angles of a triangle sum to 180°

• Interior angles of a quadrilateral sum to 360°

Parallel Lines

Alternate angles (Z-angles) are equal

Corresponding angles (F-angles) are equal

Co-interior angles (C-angles) sum to 180°

Polygon Angle Sums

Interior angles of an n-sided polygon = (n − 2) × 180°
Each exterior angle of a regular polygon = 360° ÷ n

🔄 Polygon Angle Calculator

✏️ Worked Example

Find the size of each interior angle of a regular octagon.

Step 1: Sum of interior angles = (8 − 2) × 180° = 1080°
Step 2: Each angle = 1080° ÷ 8 = 135° ✓

4.6 Circle Definitions & Theorems

💡 Key Concept — Circle Parts

  • Radius — centre to circumference
  • Diameter — through centre, = 2r
  • Chord — line segment joining two points on circumference
  • Tangent — touches circle at one point, perpendicular to radius
  • Arc — part of the circumference
  • Sector — "pizza slice" (two radii + arc)
  • Segment — region between chord and arc

Circle Theorems Higher

1. Angle at centre = 2 × angle at circumference

2. Angle in a semicircle = 90°

3. Angles in the same segment are equal

4. Opposite angles of a cyclic quadrilateral sum to 180°

5. Tangent is perpendicular to radius at point of contact

6. Tangents from an external point are equal in length

7. Perpendicular from centre to chord bisects the chord

8. Alternate segment theorem

✏️ Worked Example

O is the centre. Angle AOB = 120°. Find angle ACB (where C is on the major arc).

Step 1: Angle at centre = 2 × angle at circumference
Step 2: Angle ACB = 120° ÷ 2 = 60° ✓

4.8 Mensuration (Area, Volume, Perimeter)

💡 Essential Formulae

Rectangle: A = l × w, P = 2(l + w)

Triangle: A = ½ × b × h

Parallelogram: A = b × h

Trapezium: A = ½(a + b) × h

Circle: C = πd = 2πr, A = πr²

Cuboid: V = l × w × h

Prism: V = cross-section area × length

Cylinder: V = πr²h

Bearings

A bearing is measured clockwise from North, written as a 3-digit number (e.g., 045°, 270°).

✏️ Worked Example

Find the area of a trapezium with parallel sides 8 cm and 14 cm, and height 5 cm.

A = ½(a + b) × h = ½(8 + 14) × 5
= ½ × 22 × 5 = 55 cm² ✓

4.9 Advanced Mensuration

💡 Key Formulae

Arc length: (θ/360) × 2πr

Sector area: (θ/360) × πr²

Sphere: V = ⁴⁄₃πr³, SA = 4πr²

Cone: V = ⅓πr²h, Curved SA = πrl (l = slant height)

Pyramid: V = ⅓ × base area × h

✏️ Worked Example

Find the arc length of a sector with radius 10 cm and angle 72°.

Arc length = (72/360) × 2π × 10
= 1/5 × 20π = 4π ≈ 12.57 cm ✓

Similar Shapes — Area & Volume Higher

If linear scale factor = k, area factor = k², volume factor = k³.

4.10 Trigonometry

💡 Key Concept — SOHCAHTOA

sin θ = Opposite / Hypotenuse | cos θ = Adjacent / Hypotenuse | tan θ = Opposite / Adjacent

Pythagoras' Theorem

a² + b² = c² (c = hypotenuse)

✏️ Worked Example — Pythagoras

Find the hypotenuse of a right-angled triangle with sides 5 cm and 12 cm.

c² = 5² + 12² = 25 + 144 = 169
c = √169 = 13 cm ✓

✏️ Worked Example — Trigonometry

In a right-angled triangle, the opposite side is 8 cm and the hypotenuse is 17 cm. Find angle θ.

sin θ = 8/17 = 0.4706...
θ = sin⁻¹(0.4706) ≈ 28.1° ✓

4.11 Vectors

💡 Key Concept

A vector has both magnitude (size) and direction. Describe translations using column vectors.

⎛ x ⎞ means x units right, y units up ⎝ y ⎠

Vector Arithmetic

Addition: Add top numbers, add bottom numbers.

Scalar multiplication: Multiply both components by the scalar.

Subtraction: a⃗ − b⃗ = a⃗ + (−b⃗)

✏️ Worked Example

If a⃗ = (3, 1) and b⃗ = (−1, 4), find 2a⃗ + b⃗.

Step 1: 2a⃗ = (6, 2)
Step 2: 2a⃗ + b⃗ = (6 + (−1), 2 + 4) = (5, 6) ✓

Vector Proofs Higher

Use vectors to prove collinearity (points on same line) or that lines are parallel (one vector is a scalar multiple of another).
