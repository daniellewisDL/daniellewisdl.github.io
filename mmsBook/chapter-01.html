Chapter One

The Mind at Work

page 5

11. Every day we make decisions. We decide what clothes we will wear; whether we will walk, cycle, or go by bus, and so on. Most of the decisions we make are important only to ourselves because they don’t affect anyone else. Sometimes, however, the decision we make on what may seem a quite trivial matter may have unexpected and far-reaching consequences.

12. When we are faced with having to make a decision we know to be important we try to consider all the facts so that our decision may be the most satisfactory one possible. This is often difficult because the more important the decision is, the more varied and numerous are the factors which have to be taken into account. Often in such circumstances we make our decision according to whether it will be good for ourselves; good for other people; or just because it is right. The reason for our decision is called our motive: this may be selfish, unselfish, or moral. When an important decision has to be made it is a great help to be guided by principles such as fairness or kindness, so that the decision we make is based upon a worthier motive than self-interest.

13. Having a clear conscience comes from knowing you have acted in a way you believe to be right even though it may be unpopular. That is why people in authority with a strong sense of duty or firm religious principles are sometimes embarrassed by those who do not share their beliefs or points of view. Making decisions which affect other people is often difficult. Parents, teachers, doctors, and judges are constantly having to face this.

14. When we make a decision we choose one thing or another; we vote or we abstain; we say ‘yes’ or ‘no’; we agree or we disagree; we accept or we reject. Making up our minds is exercising this freedom of choice which we could describe as positive or negative. We could therefore indicate our choice by means of a + or − or we could use a tick or a cross. In Chapterpage 6 Four we will discuss a special kind of algebra called the Algebra of Sentences or Propositional Algebra. This is a method of expressing in symbols the relationship between a proposed decision and the conditions affecting it. When the conditions are favourable the proposed course of action is made ‘effective’—when they are unfavourable it is rendered ineffective. An example will make this clear.

If I say I will go to the coast for the day provided the car is repaired and the weather is fine, I am making two conditions upon which my proposal to go to the sea will depend.

The following sets of circumstances are possible:

Table 1
 ConditionsPropositionSymbol
[1]Car repaired: weather wetGo to the sea?0
[2]Car repaired: weather fineGo to the sea?1
[3]Car faulty: weather wetGo to the sea?0
[4]Car faulty: weather fineGo to the sea?0

Only the combination of circumstances shown at [2] is effective in making the decision positive.

In Propositional Algebra the symbols 1 or 0 are used to indicate the effect upon the original proposition.

Sometimes the words ‘True’ and ‘False’ are used to describe the effect combined conditions have upon the original proposition.

We shall see later the reason for using the symbols 1 and 0.
