89. As we noted in Section 20, Chapter Two, one of the most important inspirations man had in the development of his mathematical ideas was the invention of the symbol for zero. It is probable that this was suggested by the empty wire on the abacus, so that it stands for the ‘empty set’. Five hundred years ago it was looked upon with suspicion, even learned men were reluctant to accept the idea of a symbol for ‘nothing’, but today the concept of 0 and 1 forms the basis of logical thinking and is the main feature of the Binary notation on which the arithmetic of Electronic Computers is built.

90. In the last chapter we examined the relationships of alternatives. That is to say, we were thinking in every case of just two conditions combined in one way or another. But in problems of industrial management, finance, electronics, and science in general; as well as in problems of housing, transport, and any situation where classification or selection is involved, there are many factors to be taken into account. Problems which consist of multiple choice elements must first be analysed and broken down into sets of alternatives. When we make a rational choice we always choose between two alternatives. If we do otherwise our choice is random and unlikely to be fully effective.

An electronic computer can ‘Compare’ two numbers, and ‘Choose’ one rather than another, and the responsibility of the ‘programmer’ is to break down the multiple choice problem which is to be dealt with into its component alternatives and translate them into the numerical form the computer is designed to handle. In the questions on Section 14 we had an example of this, and in this chapter we will carry the technique a little farther.

91. The model we used for two combined conditions was two interlocked circles. Three conditions will need three circles. When two circles were interlocked they formed four distinctpage 90 areas, 2² = 4 areas. With three circles, 2³ = 8 areas. But 2⁴ = 16 areas, and we might expect there to be 2⁴ patterns. But 2⁸ = 256, which seems a great many patterns out of such a simple diagram.

In the previous chapter we found each different pattern represented a different combination of the two conditions. If this is true of the present case it means that three conditions may be combined in two hundred and fifty-six different ways. We must investigate this further.

92. The scientific method of investigation is first to observe behaviour, then to make an easily understood model of the situation and experiment on it. From this a working rule (called an hypothesis) is found, and then the hypothesis is tested by further experiment. Even then we should be cautious. To the true scientist all knowledge is constantly under review. The most we can ever do is to find an explanation which best fits the facts. But the best fit is only an approximation in the light of our present understanding. For hundreds of years men were able to ‘explain’ the movement of the Solar System by considering the Earth as the centre around which the rest revolved. Believing Man to be the most important creature in the Universe, they easily allowed their observations to persuade them the Earth occupied the central position. But the scientific mind constantly searches for what it knows it can never find—the complete truth. We must always be prepared to find out that we were wrong.

94. We can test the hypothesis of the last paragraph to our problem in this way. By enclosing an area we divide the surface into two parts—inside and outside. Where another area overlaps the first one, it must cut it into two parts, and at the same time it must form a new inside and outside out of the surrounding area. Thus, each added area which intersects all the previous ones doubles the number of areas in the diagram (see diagrams 3 and 4). Each circle represents a condition and is an element of a set which clearly accumulates by the rule we found, i.e. 2ⁿ.page 92 This number of areas then becomes the element of another set which is subject to the grouping law (like the boys going to the dentist). Diagrams as a means of explaining mathematical ideas are very useful, but they have their limitations. Sooner or later the diagram becomes more confusing than the idea it was meant to clarify. Here is a typical example! When we first learn about indices and ‘raising to powers’, the expressions ‘squared’ and ‘cubed’ are often used and we are shown models like these:

But these are limited in their application, for we cannot visualise n⁴ or any greater ‘dimension’. We may think of 5² as a point in space, but we cannot imagine 5³, it would have to be space turned inside out. Nevertheless, although powers <0 and >3 are unreal as dimensions, point, line, area, and solid are useful ways of illustrating n⁰, n¹, n², and n³. Mathematics is the study of abstract ideas, and to get the most out of it we should use diagrams only until we have grasped the underlying notion, so that we may then discard the model and develop further the mental concepts we have formed.

100. You are now probably saying to yourself, ‘What a clumsy way of deciding what vegetables to order. If you had to make out a Truth Table and draw a diagram before serving the meal, it would be spoiled before you got it. Anybody wouldpage 98 know right away that she wouldn’t have either carrots or turnips singly, or either of them with peas.’

And, of course, you are quite right. Anybody would know because as we said at the beginning of the book, our minds are quite wonderful in their ability to analyse the factors of a situation and out of them build a rational choice. But the question we are discussing is how to do this kind of thing with machines. The explanation is that we did not ‘programme’ the problem properly. We had not taken into account that Jean will only have carrots or turnips if they are mixed, or either of them with peas; we must be certain to exclude them, separately. The Algebra is not [AB] ∨ C it is [AB] ∨ [A′B′C].

101. This Algebra is designed for machines. Machines cannot think, they can only obey, and their operation is controlled by valves or transistors and the circuits through which the electricity flows. Computers are amazing devices. They can work on problems for days without suffering fatigue and they are incredibly fast in their operation. But it is the human mind of the ‘programmer’ which has to do all their true thinking for them.

102. The programming of a computer is a skilled job. The programmer must understand the problem thoroughly so that he may set out a series of instructions for the machine to follow. He must be careful not to omit any essential step however small, because if he does, the machine, blindly following his plan, will arrive at the wrong result. At the same time, a good programmer tries not to put in any unnecessary instructions.

Look at the programme called ‘Getting up and going to work’ (Fig. 66). I hope you find it amusing and instructive. You will notice that the instructions are written in two kinds of frames. This is to distinguish the two kinds of operations which the computer can perform. When it is a straightforward arithmetical process, the instruction is put in a rectangular frame, but when there has to be a choice made, the instruction is put in a rhomboidal or diamond-shaped frame so that the programmer can easily distinguish them when he is checking the programme. These always indicate a Yes/No situation and I have shown this by using the symbols 1 and 0.page 99 Trace the circuit in a number of different ways by sometimes following the ‘yes’ path, and at other times following the ‘no’ path. The programme as it is written is faulty. Perhaps you would like to discover in what way.

Try to make out a programme for a familiar task like ‘Washing up’ or ‘Cooking a meal’. Don’t forget the golden rule that no essential step must be left out. If you can manage to include some ‘choice’ questions, these will make your programme seem more like the real thing.

page 124 [4] The Tower of Brahma. This is a very ancient puzzle and I quote an interesting legend about it from Mathematical Recreations and Essays by W. W. Rouse Ball.

‘In the great temple at Benares beneath the dome which marks the centre of the world, rests a brass plate in which are fixed three diamond needles, each a cubit high and as thick as the body of a bee. On one of these needles, at the creation, God placed sixty-four discs of pure gold, the largest disc resting on the brass plate and the others getting smaller and smaller up to the top one. This is the Tower of Brahma. Day and night unceasingly, the priests transfer the discs from one diamond needle to another, according to the fixed and immutable laws of Brahma, which require that the priest on duty must not move more than one disc at a time, and that he must place this disc on a needle so that there is no smaller disc below it. When the sixty-four discs shall have thus been transferred from the needle on which, at the creation, God placed them, to one of the other needles, tower, temple, and Brahmans alike will crumble into dust, and with a thunder-clap the world will vanish.’
