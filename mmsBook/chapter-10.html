In this final chapter we will see how many of the ideas we have discussed can be applied to his latest mathematical tool, the electronic computer installation. It is called an installation because as you see it is not one machine but a number of linked units. Below I have used a diagrammatic lay-out to show you how information is transferred from one part of the system to another.

124. Information exchange in a computer system. page 151 These units are connected by electric cable and in a large installation would be housed in one room. It is possible to have some of the units hundreds of miles away in Branch offices which are thus able to make use of the computer.

125. You will have noticed in the diagrammatic lay-out of a computer installation that the actual calculator is only one part of it. It is, of course, a very important part, for it is the unit which is capable of high-speed operation. It can handle over 100,000 letter or number characters per second and could be designed to operate even faster. There are two points in the system where the processes are slowed down. These are the input and the output units, which in one way or another rely on manual intervention.

The problem is essentially one of communication. We have to keep in mind that we and the machine normally use different languages, and before we can give it instructions they must first be transformed into machine language; and in the same way, the results it obtains have to be translated into our language before they can be made use of.

126. Throughout this book there have been two main themes. One was the logic of choice, and the other the representation of numbers by symbols. Regarding the first, we saw that a rational choice is possible only between two alternatives, and the decision can be indicated by the symbols 1 and 0. We have seen, too, that the digits 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 0 are not the only ones by which we may record numbers of things, and the determining factor is the base or group we choose. There are advantages in using bases other than ten for some purposes, and this is particularly true of the Binary system. In this it is possible to represent any number by means of only two symbols, 1 and 0. Thus, we come to the heart of the matter, for these two symbols precisely match the two states on/off of an electrical circuit, and in this way, modern electronic computers are ideal instruments for dealing with two-state logical situations and numerical calculations.

127. When you or I have a problem to solve, a variety of activities are set in motion. If the problem is a familiar one, such as cutting some bread and butter, we organise our actions so as to produce the desired result. We go to the container and get out the bread; we get the butter from the larder: and the knifepage 152 from the cutlery drawer; and we get a plate to hold the bread and butter after it has been prepared.

Because these activities are familiar we don’t appreciate how complex the mental processes are that govern our behaviour. Have you ever been asked to prepare bread and butter in a strange house? If you have you will have realised how many decisions you have to make, and how impossible the task may be if you are unable to locate only one of the necessary items. The sequence of your behaviour could be described as:

  1. The comprehension of what is required.
  2. The collection of the necessary items.
  3. The performance of the task.

For all these you rely primarily upon your memory. You know what the task is, what you will need, and how to do it. In a strange house you would still know all this, what you may not know is where the items are to be found. Imagine you are in the kitchen of your aunt’s new house and you have been asked to cut the bread and butter. You can’t find the bread-knife and when you ask your aunt, who is in the garden, she says, ‘It is in the middle section of the third drawer.’ When you look you find there are three sections containing:

[1] A bread-knife [2] A spoon [3] A fish-slice

You modify your aunt’s instruction and take out the bread-knife from the first section. This is because you are human and intelligent. The computer would have used the spoon.

128. A computer is a poor substitute for the human brain. Strictly speaking, it has no overall conception of a problem: it can gather together the necessary items only if it is told exactly what they are and where they may be found each time. When these two ‘intelligent’ provisions have been made for it by the human beings who control it, then it can usually perform the mechanical task much faster than they could hope to. Of course, one would never expect a computer to cut bread and butter.

129. page 153 What is called the ‘memory’ of a computer is a store or register of numbers which are required for a particular problem. If, for instance, the computer were engaged on working out wages, in the ‘memory’ would be stored the rates of pay, the amounts of National Insurance, the Income Tax, and other permanent details relating to all the persons employed by that particular firm. These details can be recorded on magnetic tape,

or on punched cards, or on punched paper tape, or on a magnetic revolving drum. What is of vital importance is that each number must have a definite location in the register (usually called its address) so that the programmer can instruct the computer precisely as to where the detail may be found.

130. If you have ever used a tape recorder you will know that every note of music and every syllable of speech is held on the magnetic tape in a certain position. By the ingenious use of the counter it is possible to locate a particular point in the recording. Numbers, stored as magnetised spots, can be located by the computer, but care must be taken to ensure they are recorded in the correct sequence, as once any particular spot has passed the reading head it will not be available again until the spool canpage 154 be re-run. This ‘slow access’ time, as it is called, applies also to punched tape, as it, too, can be ‘read’ only in a set order. Information is more readily available from a revolving drum. Rotating at 5,000 revs. per second it makes any piece of information available 5,000 times every second.

Drum storage is by means of magnetised spots which are located by ‘track’ and item order (see Fig. 91). Illustrations of individual units appear between pages 150 and 151.

131. All digital computers operate in response to ‘words’ expressed in Binary digits. In the machine these bits are held as patterns of magnetism or as two-state switching devices. They can be transferred from one part of the machine to another as electronic pulses at the rate of 1,000 per second (kilocycles) or, sometimes, 1,000,000 per second (megacycles). Many codes are used as machine language. The simplest binary coding used here could be as many as 40 bits to a word; the maximum decimal capacity is thus 1,099,511,627,775.

  page 155 This ‘word’ represents the total shown, but part of the word is generally an instruction as well as a quantity. When a Decimal/Binary coding is used, each Decimal digit consists of 4 bits read simultaneously, each column being read in sequence. A system of punched holes is used in this way: the punched hole represents 1, while the unpunched represents 0 in the appropriate position. The maximum decimal capacity equals 999,999,999,999.

139. page 161 The number-code which forms the basis of machine language, as we have seen, can take many forms. It may be a pattern of magnetic spots on tape or metallic sheets or on the surface of a revolving drum. It may be a pattern of holes punched into cards or paper tape. Another method, which is particularly suitable for sorting and classifying purposes, is to punch the holes in the edge of index cards. It is usual then to let the holes represent zero. To represent 1 the hole is continued to the edge of the card forming a slot. The cards are in decks of 256 and each card is identified by a pattern of holes and slots cut into its top edge.

You will recall that when three circles were interlocked they formed eight areas, and the eight areas made the different pattern possibilities (see Section 94, Example 5). In the same way, eight holes are sufficient to identify any one of 256 cards. Oncepage 162 the correct card has been selected it can be carried round a read/write mechanism which will transmit information to the computer or record new information from the computer on to the card.

143. In a computer system, the sorting of a deck of cards into a special order, or the isolation of a particular card is donepage 164 electronically and at high speed. When we copy this operation by hand it will seem slow and clumsy.

Example 1. Imagine the cards are punched to show the personal details of a firm’s employees. When a man is married the first hole is slotted; and the second hole is slotted when he has completed fifteen years service. A manager is to be appointed to a new branch where it will be necessary to entertain important clients. The Directors of the firm decide that the new manager shall be appointed from among the men on their staff who are married and have atpage 165 least fifteen years service. Eligible candidates could be selected by: Push the needle through hole A and lift. The cards of all the married men will fall out. Take these cards and push the needle through hole B. This time the cards which fall out will be of married men with fifteen years service. Doing this to our small set produces only two cards, Nos. 6 and 7.

Example 2. There has been a robbery at a jeweller’s. An assistant describes one of the men as having bright-red hair which may have been a wig, although it was close-fitting. At Scotland Yard the cards of all known criminals are put through the selector. This time we have to select all red-haired or bald men. Let A and B be the appropriate hole/slot positions. Pushing the needle through A will allow the cards of red-haired men to be isolated. Pushing it through hole B of the rest of the cards will find additionally the cards of bald-headed men. When we try this we get cards Nos. 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 and 7. There is something odd about the last two cards. Can you say what it is?

146. Let us use Example 2 from Section 143 as a demonstration of the way to select a case of the exclusive disjunction, i.e. A ⊻ B. When we push the needle through A, the cards divide into sub-sets, those containing A and those not containing A. If we take the first lot and reject any containing B by pushing the needle through B and retaining only those which do not fall, we will have one lot which are A but not B, i.e. AB′. We know the second lot does not contain any A because we removed them all the first time. What we want from this sub-set is all the cards which are B. Those which fall are A′B. The two selections are then merged. You may remember from Section 86 or Section 101 that A ⊻ B = [AB′ ∨ A′B]. This equivalence is confirmed by the behaviour of the cards. When we thus operate on our set cards Nos. 6 and 7 are rejected.

147. It would be useful to make a list of this selection procedure for future reference.

  1. Perform an operation on one condition at a time (making a rational rather than a random choice).
  2. If the effective condition is positive, the cards required fall.
  3. If the relationship is ∧, perform the second operation on the effective cards only.page 167
  4. If the relationship is ∨, perform the second operation on the previously ineffective cards, and combine those which were selected first time with those which were selected this time.
  5. If the required relationship is ⊻, select the effective cards of the first condition and apply to them the complement of the second condition. Select the effective cards from the first lot of rejects by applying to them the second condition. Merge both.
  6. If brackets are employed in the algebraic relationship, find the effective cards from the bracketed conditions before applying the external condition.

148. As we haven’t demonstrated the card selection procedure for bracketed relationships, let us try one now. If the regulations governing the eligibility for housing tenancy were that ‘applicants must be married and either have been born in the neighbourhood or have resided there for not less than five years continuously up to the time of applying’ this would be a case of A ∧ [B ∨ C]. Assume that applications are filed by means of a punched-card system in which the first hole is slotted for married applicants, the second hole is slotted if the applicant is a native of the neighbourhood and the third hole is slotted after five years’ continuous residence. When a housing estate is ready for occupation the list of eligible persons could be compiled by:

  1. Spearing hole B and putting those which fall on one side.†
  2. Spearing hole C in the remaining cards and combining those which fall this time with those which fell the first time.†
  3. Taking the combined effective sub-sets and spearing hole A. Only those which fall this time are eligible.†

With our test pack we get cards Nos. 5, 6 and 7.

† In a data processing installation this would be done by a card-sorting machine which ‘senses’ the presence of a hole in the card.
